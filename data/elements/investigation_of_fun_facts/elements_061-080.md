## Element 61 — Pm — Promethium
- **fact_a_true:** "Promethium is named after a hero from Greek myths who gave fire to people."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Promethium IS named after Prometheus, the Greek Titan who stole fire and gave it to humanity. Marinsky, Glendenin, and Coryell named it in 1945.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Promethium is named after Berkeley, California, where scientists first made it."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That story belongs to Berkelium (Bk-97), not Promethium. Promethium was first isolated at Oak Ridge, Tennessee.
  - Concern: HIGH — Berkelium row #97 makes this exact correct claim, so kid hearing both rows could confidently misattribute "Berkeley = Promethium".

## Element 62 — Sm — Samarium
- **fact_a_true:** "Samarium makes powerful magnets that still work even when very hot."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Samarium-cobalt (SmCo) magnets are well known for retaining magnetization at high temperatures (up to ~350°C), which is why they're used in aerospace and military applications.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Samarium is the bright yellow stuff near volcanoes, and it smells like rotten eggs."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's sulfur (S-16). Samarium is a silvery-white rare-earth metal, not a yellow non-metal, and doesn't smell.
  - Concern: HIGH — Sulfur row #16 makes this exact claim as its fact_a. The mislearning would directly cross-contaminate two element identities.

## Element 63 — Eu — Europium
- **fact_a_true:** "Europium makes the red color in TV screens and the glow in some banknotes."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Europium-doped phosphors (Eu³⁺ for red, Eu²⁺ for blue) are standard in CRT/LCD red phosphors, and europium fluorescence is used as an anti-counterfeit feature in euro banknotes.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Europium is added to table salt to help your body grow strong."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's iodine (I-53). Europium is not added to food and is not a dietary nutrient.
  - Concern: HIGH — Iodine row #53 makes this exact claim. Also a mild safety concern: a kid might think rare-earth metals are edible.

## Element 64 — Gd — Gadolinium

  - Reason: Gadolinium-based contrast agents (e.g., Gd-DTPA) are the standard MRI contrast medium due to Gd³⁺'s seven unpaired electrons.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Gadolinium is named after Thor, the Norse god of thunder."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Gadolinium is named after Finnish chemist Johan Gadolin. Thorium (Th-90) is the one named after Thor.
  - Concern: HIGH — Thorium row #90 makes this exact claim. Direct cross-attribution risk.

  - Concern: NONE

## Element 66 — Dy — Dysprosium
- **fact_a_true:** "Dysprosium makes magnets that don't lose their pull even when very hot."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Dysprosium is added to neodymium magnets specifically to raise their Curie temperature and prevent demagnetization at high operating temperatures (e.g., in electric-vehicle motors and wind turbines).
  - Concern: LOW — Overlaps conceptually with Samarium's heat-resistant magnet claim (#62), but the use-case is distinct (dopant vs. base alloy). Still, two adjacent rows both say "magnets that work when hot" — kid will conflate.
- **fact_b_false:** "Dysprosium cleans up car exhaust so the air stays cleaner to breathe."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's palladium (Pd-46), or platinum/rhodium in catalytic converters. Dysprosium has no role in vehicle emission control.
  - Concern: HIGH — Palladium row #46 makes this exact claim.

## Element 67 — Ho — Holmium
- **fact_a_true:** "Holmium creates the strongest magnetic fields of any natural element."
  - Verdict: TRUE (verified)
  - Reason: Holmium has the highest magnetic permeability and magnetic moment (10.6 μB) of any element, so it's used as pole pieces in the strongest static-magnet setups. Note: holmium is paramagnetic at room temperature — it becomes ferromagnetic only below −254°C. The "creates" wording is slightly imprecise (holmium concentrates field rather than generates it), but it's commonly stated this way in popular sources.
  - Concern: MED — Overlaps with Neodymium row #60's "strongest magnets on Earth" claim. Both are technically correct under different definitions (Nd-Fe-B = strongest permanent magnet at room T; Ho = highest permeability, used inside the strongest research magnets). A 4yo cannot distinguish — will hear "strongest magnet" twice and not know which is which.
- **fact_b_false:** "Holmium grows into beautiful rainbow staircase crystals that look like tiny castles."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's bismuth (Bi-83). Holmium crystals are not famous for hopper/staircase iridescent growth.
  - Concern: HIGH — Bismuth row #83 makes this exact claim. The rainbow-staircase image is visually iconic and a kid will lock it onto whichever element they hear first.

## Element 68 — Er — Erbium
- **fact_a_true:** "Erbium colors glass and ceramics a pretty pink and helps light travel through cables."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Erbium³⁺ colors glass pink/rose, and erbium-doped fiber amplifiers (EDFAs) are the workhorse of long-haul telecom fiber optics at 1.5 μm.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Erbium makes balloons float and makes voices sound funny when you breathe it in."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's helium (He-2). Erbium is a solid metal, not a low-density gas. Erbium is also toxic; a "breathe it in" suggestion is a small but real safety concern.
  - Concern: HIGH — Helium row #2 makes this exact claim, and the "breathe it in" framing is one a kid may try to mimic.

## Element 69 — Tm — Thulium
- **fact_a_true:** "Thulium glows blue in some special lasers and was once the rarest of the rare earths."
  - Verdict: MISLEADING (mostly TRUE with a slight stretch)
  - Reason: Thulium does fluoresce blue under UV and is used in flat-panel displays. However, Tm:YAG lasers actually emit in the near-IR (~2 μm), not blue. Thulium is also commonly described as "the rarest of the stable lanthanides" — promethium is rarer but is radioactive/unstable, so "once the rarest of the rare earths" is defensible. The blue-laser phrasing conflates fluorescence color with laser emission color.
  - Concern: LOW — A 4yo doesn't know laser wavelengths; the "blue glow" mental image is fine and is in fact correct for thulium phosphors.
- **fact_b_false:** "Thulium helps make the bright red dots on old TV screens."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's yttrium (Y-39) with europium (Eu-63) — yttrium oxide doped with europium is the classic red CRT phosphor. Thulium phosphors are blue.
  - Concern: HIGH — Yttrium row #39 makes this exact claim. Direct contradiction to thulium's actual blue color makes this especially confusing.

## Element 70 — Yb — Ytterbium
- **fact_a_true:** "Ytterbium helps make atomic clocks that keep time more precisely than ever before."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Ytterbium optical lattice clocks (NIST Yb clocks) are among the most accurate timepieces ever built, with fractional uncertainty below 10⁻¹⁸. They've surpassed cesium-based clocks in stability.
  - Concern: LOW — Overlaps with Cesium row #55's "most accurate clocks" claim. Both true, but a kid will hear "best clock" twice. This is a defensible duplication since both are real.
- **fact_b_false:** "Ytterbium is the densest natural element, so a small chunk feels surprisingly heavy."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's osmium (Os-76). Ytterbium's density is only ~6.97 g/cm³, which is unusually LOW for a lanthanide (less dense than iron). Osmium is ~22.59 g/cm³.
  - Concern: HIGH — Osmium row #76 makes this exact claim. Ytterbium is almost the opposite of dense for a heavy-element; it's the WORST candidate to swap with osmium.

## Element 71 — Lu — Lutetium
- **fact_a_true:** "Lutetium is one of the rarest and most expensive metals to dig out of the ground."
  - Verdict: MISLEADING (verified)
  - Reason: Lutetium IS one of the most expensive rare earths to extract (around $10,000/kg, due to its low concentration and difficult separation from other heavy rare earths). However, rhodium is the most expensive non-synthetic element overall by mass — significantly more expensive than lutetium. The phrasing "one of the rarest and most expensive" is defensible (it doesn't claim THE most expensive), but the Rhodium row #45 also describes rhodium as "one of the rarest metals," so the two rows are both defensibly true and not contradictory.
  - Concern: LOW — The wording "one of the rarest" is hedged enough to be safe. A 4yo won't try to rank-order.
- **fact_b_false:** "Lutetium is named after Nihon, which is the Japanese word for Japan."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's nihonium (Nh-113). Lutetium is named after Lutetia, the Latin name for Paris.
  - Concern: HIGH — Nihonium row #113 makes this exact claim. Direct cross-contamination.

## Element 80 — Hg — Mercury

  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Mercury (melting point −38.83°C) is the only metallic element that is liquid at standard room temperature. (Gallium, francium, cesium, rubidium melt slightly above room temp and only become liquid on a warm day or with body heat.) The "only" wording matches common usage.
  - Concern: LOW — Mild safety concern: mercury is highly toxic. The "silver water" framing is appealing and could prompt a curious kid to want to touch it. - **fact_b_false:** "Mercury is named after the planet Uranus and powers nuclear reactors that make electricity."
  - Verdict: FALSE

  - Concern: HIGH — Uranium row #92 makes this exact claim. Especially confusing because mercury IS named after a planet, just a different one. A 4yo who just learned "mercury = a planet" may not catch which planet.

### Recommended N1 operator action on rows 61–80
All 20 fact_a strings can advance to `source: operator`. The **fact_b strings are the dangerous ones** — every single one is structurally a "TRUE statement about a different element" swap. This is a deliberate pattern in the corpus and the kid risk is high because the correct attribution is also in the same training set. Two paths:

1. **Keep as-is** and rely on the microgame loop never reading fact_b aloud to the kid (use it only as a distractor button the kid does NOT pick).
2. **Rewrite fact_b strings** to be obviously-absurd-to-an-adult false claims (e.g., "Promethium makes pancakes fluffy") rather than plausible-but-misattributed claims. This shifts the failure mode from "kid mislearns which element does what" to "kid laughs at a silly claim."

The choice depends on how the microgame uses these fields. If fact_b is ever spoken to the kid, recommend path 2.

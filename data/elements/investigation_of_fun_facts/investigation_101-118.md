# Investigation: elements 101-118 (Mendelevium → Oganesson)

## #101 Mendelevium (Md-101)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Mendelevium is named after the scientist who first organized the periodic table.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named after Dmitri Mendeleev, who published the first widely-recognized periodic table in 1869. Age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Mendelevium was the first element ever made by people, in a lab in 1937.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Technetium (Tc-43)
- Notes: Mendelevium was synthesized in 1955. The "first element made by people, in a lab in 1937" describes Technetium (Perrier & Segrè, 1937). Year + claim are tight enough that a child could mislearn the date, but the misattribution is clean.

## #102 Nobelium (No-102)

- Verdict: TRUE

**fact_b (claimed false):** Nobelium is the only metal that's liquid at room temperature, like silver water.

## #104 Rutherfordium (Rf-104)

## #111 Roentgenium (Rg-111)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Roentgenium is named after Wilhelm Roentgen, who discovered X-rays.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Correct (1895 X-ray discovery). Age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Roentgenium colors special goggles green so welders can see safely through bright sparks.
- Verdict: AMBIGUOUS
- Belongs to: not cleanly true of any one element
- Notes: Welding-goggle green tint is typically produced by iron and chromium oxides in the glass, with some lenses using rare-earth additions (didymium = Nd + Pr — but didymium glass actually appears pink/violet and is for glassblowers/lampworkers, not welders). The claim doesn't map to a single canonical element, so it works as a generic distractor with no sticky misattribution risk — but it also doesn't follow the audit's "recycled true-fact" pattern. Defensible to keep; consider replacing with a cleaner recycled-true-fact distractor for consistency.

## #117 Tennessine (Ts-117)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Tennessine is named after the U.S. state of Tennessee, where some of the work happened.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Correct (Oak Ridge National Lab + Vanderbilt + UT). Age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Tennessine is one of the rarest metals and gives jewelry its bright silvery shine.

## #118 Oganesson (Og-118)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Oganesson is the heaviest element ever made and is named after Yuri Oganessian.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Correct as of 2026 — Og is the highest atomic number currently confirmed (Z=118). Named after living scientist Yuri Oganessian. Age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Oganesson is one of the heaviest common metals and was used in pencils long ago.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Lead (Pb-82)
- Notes: "Lead pencils" is the historical misnomer (pencils have always used graphite, but the name "lead" stuck and lead was a common heavy metal). Doubly wrong about Og: Oganesson is classified as a noble gas, not a metal, and is not "common" — only a handful of atoms have ever existed. The lead-pencil cue lands clearly on Pb, so no sticky misattribution to Og.

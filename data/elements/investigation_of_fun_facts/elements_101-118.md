## Element 101 — Md — Mendelevium
- **fact_a_true:** "Mendelevium is named after the scientist who first organized the periodic table."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Mendelevium (1955, Berkeley) is named after Dmitri Mendeleev, who published the
    1869 periodic table generally credited as "the first organized" version.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Mendelevium was the first element ever made by people, in a lab in 1937."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's TECHNETIUM (#43, 1937, Perrier & Segrè). Md was first synthesized in 1955.
  - Concern: HIGH — perfectly plausible-sounding, and a kid who hears it will conflate Md with
    the real "first synthetic element" story. Recycled directly from row #43's true fact.

## Element 102 — No — Nobelium
- **fact_a_true:** "Nobelium is named after Alfred Nobel, who started the Nobel Prizes."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Standard IUPAC etymology — named for Alfred Nobel (dynamite inventor, founder of
    the Nobel Prize).
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Nobelium is the only metal that's liquid at room temperature, like silver water."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's MERCURY (#80). Nobelium is a synthetic superheavy with a half-life of
    minutes; "room temperature" isn't really meaningful and it is certainly not a liquid metal.
  - Concern: HIGH — sounds magical and memorable; kid will mislearn "Nobelium = liquid metal"
    instead of mercury.

  - Concern: NONE — though note GSI is technically in Darmstadt (a city in Hesse), so the

- **fact_b_false:** "Hassium makes magnets that don't lose their pull even when very hot."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's DYSPROSIUM (#66) — the high-temperature-magnet rare earth. Also overlaps
    with SAMARIUM (#62, "powerful magnets that still work even when very hot"). Hs is a
    transient synthetic; no magnet application.
  - Concern: HIGH — magnets are a sticky concept; will mis-attribute.

# Investigation: Elements 21–40 (Scandium → Zirconium)

Generated 2026-05-18. Reviewer: agent-2.

## Element 21 — Sc — Scandium
- **fact_a_true:** "Scandium makes baseball bats and bicycle frames super light and strong."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Sc–Al alloys (e.g., the original Easton ScandiumAl bats, high-end bike frames like Reynolds 853-style Sc-Al tubing) are a real and well-known use.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Scandium is a metalloid that helps make computer chips and tiny LED lights."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Scandium is a transition metal (group 3), NOT a metalloid. Metalloids on that staircase (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te) are the chip/LED stars. This is exactly the "plausible but wrong-for-this-element" pattern; a kid would mislearn Sc's category.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 22 — Ti — Titanium
- **fact_a_true:** "Titanium is so strong and light that airplanes, ships, and even toothpaste use it."
  - Verdict: MISLEADING
  - Reason: Airplanes and (some) ship components do use titanium metal, but toothpaste contains titanium DIOXIDE (TiO2) as a white pigment, not titanium metal — and TiO2 is being phased out in many regions. The "use it" framing conflates the metal with a compound. Minor for a 4yo, but a literal reading mis-teaches.
  - Concern: LOW
- **fact_b_false:** "Titanium helps make atomic clocks that keep time more precisely than ever before."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Atomic clocks use cesium (the SI second is defined by Cs-133), rubidium, or — for next-gen optical clocks — ytterbium/strontium/aluminum-ion. Titanium has no role here. The claim is true of Yb (row 70).
  - Concern: MED

## Element 23 — V — Vanadium
- **fact_a_true:** "Vanadium makes steel tools extra tough, like the wrenches in a car mechanic's shop."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Chrome-vanadium steel (Cr-V) is a standard tool-steel formulation used in wrenches, sockets, and hand tools.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Vanadium is named after Niels Bohr, who imagined how electrons orbit atoms."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Vanadium is named after Vanadís (an alternate name for the Norse goddess Freyja), referring to its beautiful colored compounds. Bohrium (Bh, 107) is the element named after Bohr.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 24 — Cr — Chromium
- **fact_a_true:** "Chromium makes the shiny silver coating on car bumpers and bicycle bells."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Chrome plating is precisely this application.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Chromium makes your blood red and is the metal inside skyscrapers and bridges."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That is IRON (hemoglobin + structural steel). Chromium is added to steel for stainless/tool steel and gives blood-red color only in chromium-VI compounds (toxic), not in human blood. Highly confusable since both metals are added to steel.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 25 — Mn — Manganese
- **fact_a_true:** "Manganese helps batteries work and gives some glass a pretty purple color."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Alkaline and Zn-C batteries use MnO2 as the cathode; manganese (as Mn^3+/Mn^4+ in glass, "glassmaker's soap") imparts amethyst-purple color.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Manganese makes the red color in TV screens and the glow in some banknotes."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: The red phosphor in CRT TVs is europium-activated (YVO4:Eu or Y2O2S:Eu), and banknote anticounterfeit phosphors are typically Eu/Tb-based. Manganese is used in some green/yellow phosphors (Mn-doped zinc silicate) but not the red-TV or banknote roles.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 26 — Fe — Iron
- **fact_a_true:** "Iron makes your blood red and is the metal inside skyscrapers and bridges."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Hemoglobin's iron gives oxygenated blood its red color; structural steel (mostly iron) frames skyscrapers and bridges.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Iron is named after Enrico Fermi, a scientist who built the first nuclear reactor."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Iron's name is prehistoric (Old English "iren"/"iron"; symbol Fe from Latin "ferrum"). Fermium (Fm, 100) is the element named after Fermi.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 27 — Co — Cobalt
- **fact_a_true:** "Cobalt makes the deep blue color in old pottery and stained-glass windows."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Cobalt blue (CoAl2O4) and smalt are the classic blue pigments used in Delft pottery, Chinese blue-and-white porcelain, and medieval stained glass.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Bananas are full of Cobalt, which helps your muscles work properly."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Bananas are famous for POTASSIUM (K), not cobalt. Cobalt is a trace nutrient (as the core of vitamin B12) but in micrograms and not from bananas. This directly inverts the row-19 potassium fact and is exactly the cross-row confusion to avoid.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 28 — Ni — Nickel
- **fact_a_true:** "Nickel is the shiny metal in the five-cent coin called a nickel."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: The US nickel coin is 25% nickel / 75% copper, and gets its common name from the metal.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Nickel makes fireworks burn bright green and helps doctors take pictures of stomachs."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Both halves are BARIUM. Barium salts (Ba(NO3)2) give green fireworks; barium sulfate is the "barium swallow" GI-imaging contrast agent. Nickel does neither.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 29 — Cu — Copper
- **fact_a_true:** "Copper turns green when it's outside for a long time, like the Statue of Liberty."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Copper develops a green patina (basic copper carbonates/sulfates) outdoors; the Statue of Liberty is the textbook example.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Copper is named after the planet Neptune, just past Uranus in our solar system."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Copper is named after Cyprus (Latin cuprum, from "aes cyprium"). Neptunium (Np, 93) is named after Neptune.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 30 — Zn — Zinc
- **fact_a_true:** "Zinc keeps cuts and scrapes from getting sore and also makes pennies feel light."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Zinc oxide is the active in diaper-rash creams and many wound ointments; US pennies have been 97.5% zinc (Cu-plated) since 1982, which is why modern pennies feel lighter than pre-1982 copper ones.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Zinc burns with a super-bright white flame, which is why old camera flashes used it."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That is MAGNESIUM (Mg flash powder, magnesium ribbon). Zinc does burn — with a bluish-green flame — but it was not the flashbulb metal.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 31 — Ga — Gallium
- **fact_a_true:** "Gallium melts in your hand because its melting point is below body temperature."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Gallium's melting point is 29.76 °C, below the ~37 °C of body heat. Classic demo.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Gallium makes touchscreens work so you can tap your tablet and phone."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: The transparent conductor in touchscreens is INDIUM tin oxide (ITO). Gallium is used in some LEDs and semiconductors (GaN, GaAs), but not the touchscreen layer. Highly confusable because indium and gallium sit next to each other on the table.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 32 — Ge — Germanium
- **fact_a_true:** "Germanium was used in the very first transistors that started the computer age."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: The 1947 Bell Labs point-contact transistor (Bardeen/Brattain/Shockley) used germanium; silicon replaced it later.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Germanium is a gas that seeps up from the ground and is checked for in basements."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That is RADON. Germanium is a solid metalloid at room temperature. A 4yo could come away thinking Ge is a household radiation hazard.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 33 — As — Arsenic
- **fact_a_true:** "Arsenic is a metalloid that helps make computer chips and tiny LED lights."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Arsenic is a metalloid; gallium-arsenide (GaAs) and aluminum-gallium-arsenide are real semiconductor / LED / laser-diode materials.
  - Concern: NONE (caveat: arsenic is also a famous poison, but this is a true industrial use)
- **fact_b_false:** "Arsenic helps make strong glass that doesn't crack when it gets hot."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Heat-resistant glass (borosilicate, e.g., Pyrex) is BORON's claim. Arsenic was used historically as a fining agent in some glass but is not what makes glass thermal-shock-resistant.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 34 — Se — Selenium
- **fact_a_true:** "Selenium helps photocopiers see and copy pictures on paper."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Selenium drums (and later organic photoconductors) were the photoreceptor in xerographic copiers — Chester Carlson's original xerography used Se.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Selenium makes steel tools tough enough to cut through other metals."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Cutting-tool toughness comes from MOLYBDENUM / TUNGSTEN / vanadium in high-speed and tool steels. Selenium is added in small amounts to some free-machining steels for chip-breaking but is not what makes a tool "cut through other metals."
  - Concern: MED

## Element 35 — Br — Bromine
- **fact_a_true:** "Bromine is one of only two liquid elements at room temperature, the other is mercury."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: At 25 °C and 1 atm, only Br and Hg are liquid elements. (Cs, Ga, Rb, Fr have melting points slightly above standard room temperature.)
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Bromine is so soft you can hammer it into sheets thin enough to see through."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Bromine is a LIQUID, not a malleable metal — you cannot hammer a liquid into sheets. The "hammer into thin sheets" claim is GOLD's (gold leaf). A 4yo could lose the basic state-of-matter for Br.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 36 — Kr — Krypton
- **fact_a_true:** "Krypton glows pale lavender in lights and shares its name with Superman's home planet."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Krypton emits a whitish/pale-lavender glow in gas-discharge tubes; Superman's home is Krypton (the element predates the comic). Greek "kryptos" = hidden.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Krypton is named after Livermore, a California lab where scientists studied it."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That is LIVERMORIUM (Lv, 116). Krypton's name is from Greek "kryptos" (hidden), because it was a hidden trace component of liquefied air.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 37 — Rb — Rubidium
- **fact_a_true:** "Rubidium is named for its ruby-red color when it burns in a flame."
  - Verdict: MISLEADING
  - Reason: The name comes from Latin "rubidus" (deep red) — but historically that referred to the dark-red spectral lines Bunsen and Kirchhoff saw via flame spectroscopy, not the bulk burning color. The flame test of Rb does appear red-violet, so the simplified "red-when-it-burns" framing is roughly defensible for a 4yo and lines up with the established convention (Cs/Rb/In/Tl were all named for their flame-spectroscopy colors).
  - Concern: LOW
- **fact_b_false:** "Rubidium is one of only two liquid elements at room temperature, the other is mercury."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Rubidium melts at 39.3 °C — slightly above standard room temperature; it's a solid at room temp (would melt in a warm hand or hot room). The two true room-temp liquid elements are bromine and mercury (row 35's fact_a). A kid could lose the row-35 anchor.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 38 — Sr — Strontium
- **fact_a_true:** "Strontium makes fireworks burn bright red, which is why holiday rockets are so colorful."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Strontium salts (SrCO3, Sr(NO3)2) are the canonical red-firework colorant.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Strontium makes super-fast computer chips run cooler and use less power."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: That's HAFNIUM — HfO2 high-k gate dielectrics in modern CPUs replaced silicon dioxide starting with Intel's 45 nm node. Strontium is used in some CRT glass and ferroelectric capacitors but is not the high-k chip story.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 39 — Y — Yttrium
- **fact_a_true:** "Yttrium helps make the bright red dots on old TV screens."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: The red CRT phosphor is Eu-doped yttrium oxysulfide (Y2O2S:Eu) or yttrium vanadate (YVO4:Eu). Y is the host lattice; Eu is the activator that emits red. Crediting Y is fair.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Yttrium is named after Lise Meitner, a scientist who studied splitting atoms."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Yttrium is named after the Swedish village Ytterby (along with terbium, erbium, and ytterbium — four elements from one mine). Meitnerium (Mt, 109) is named after Meitner.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 40 — Zr — Zirconium
- **fact_a_true:** "Zirconium makes fake diamonds that sparkle just like real ones in jewelry."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Cubic zirconia (ZrO2 in cubic crystal form) is the standard diamond simulant in costume jewelry.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Zirconium is the shiny metal in the five-cent coin called a Zirconium."
  - Verdict: FALSE
  - Reason: Self-referential nonsense — the five-cent coin is the nickel, named after nickel (row 28). No coin is called a "zirconium." The structure makes the fabrication obvious, but a 4yo might just hear "Zirconium = coin" and store that.
  - Concern: MED

# Investigation: elements 1-20 (Hydrogen → Calcium)

## #1 Hydrogen (H-1)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Hydrogen is the lightest element and makes up most of the sun.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Hydrogen is element #1, lowest atomic mass, and the Sun is ~73% hydrogen by mass. Age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Hydrogen makes balloons float and makes voices sound funny when you breathe it in.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Helium (recycled from row #2 fact_a)
- Notes: Physically, hydrogen WOULD make a balloon float (it's even lighter than helium) and would also raise voice pitch by the same mechanism (lower gas density → faster speed of sound). But party balloons use helium because hydrogen is flammable. A 4yo hearing this could mislearn the canonical "balloons = helium" association, and could form a dangerous belief about breathing hydrogen.

## #2 Helium (He-2)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Helium makes balloons float and makes voices sound funny when you breathe it in.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Standard, accurate. Worth noting "funny voice" trick can cause asphyxiation if overdone, but the statement itself is fine for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Helium makes fireworks burn bright red, which is why holiday rockets are so colorful.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Strontium (bright red) or Lithium (red/crimson). Helium is inert and is not used to color fireworks.
- Notes: Cleanly false; a child won't easily verify which element actually does it, so low mis-teach risk for the negative association.

## #3 Lithium (Li-3)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Lithium is the lightest metal and powers many phones, tablets, and electric cars.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Lithium is the least-dense solid metal (0.534 g/cm³). Li-ion batteries power consumer electronics and EVs.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Lithium helps jet engines spin hot and fast without melting.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Nickel (superalloys, Inconel) or Titanium — the heat-resistant jet-engine metals. Tungsten/rhenium also fit.
- Notes: Lithium has a low melting point (~180°C) and would be one of the worst choices. Plausibly recycled from a Ni/Ti/W fact.

## #4 Beryllium (Be-4)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Beryllium is a strong, light metal that helps build airplanes and spaceships.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Be is used in aerospace (mirrors on JWST, structural alloys). Slightly euphemistic — it's also highly toxic — but fine for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Beryllium is named after Nicolaus Copernicus, who taught that Earth circles the sun.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Copernicium (Cn-112)
- Notes: Beryllium is named after the mineral beryl. Copernicus naming → Cn. Low mis-teach risk; child won't associate the wrong scientist with Be.

## #5 Boron (B-5)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Boron helps make strong glass that doesn't crack when it gets hot.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Borosilicate glass (Pyrex) uses boron oxide for low thermal expansion. Age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Boron melts in your hand because its melting point is below body temperature.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Gallium (melting point ~29.8°C, below body temp)
- Notes: Boron's actual melting point is ~2076°C — extremely high. Cleanly false, classic gallium party trick.

## #6 Carbon (C-6)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Carbon is in every living thing on Earth, from trees to people to your pet dog.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Carbon is the backbone of all known organic life. Excellent kid-level statement.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Carbon colors glass and ceramics a pretty pink and helps light travel through cables.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Erbium (pink glass/ceramics tint AND erbium-doped fiber amplifiers for telecom). A nearly-perfect match.
- Notes: Clean false attribution. Carbon does appear in optical contexts (graphite, diamond) but not pink coloration or fiber-optic light transmission.

## #7 Nitrogen (N-7)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Most of the air we breathe is nitrogen, even more than oxygen.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Air is ~78% N2, ~21% O2. Accurate and useful for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Nitrogen is named after Dubna, a Russian town where scientists made it.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Dubnium (Db-105)
- Notes: Nitrogen is named from Greek "nitron" (soda/saltpetre) + "-gen" (forming). Cleanly false; low mis-teach risk.

## #8 Oxygen (O-8)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Oxygen is the part of the air our lungs use to keep us alive.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Accurate. Slightly simplified — oxygen is what cells use, not "lungs use" directly — but acceptable for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Oxygen makes the shiny silver coating on car bumpers and bicycle bells.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Chromium (chrome plating) — recycled from row #24 fact_a
- Notes: Oxygen actually causes rust/tarnish on metals, the opposite of a shiny plating. Cleanly false.

## #9 Fluorine (F-9)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Fluorine helps make toothpaste that keeps your teeth strong.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Fluoride (the F⁻ ion) in toothpaste strengthens enamel. Standard kid-level fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Fluorine is named after a hero from Greek myths who gave fire to people.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Promethium (Pm-61, named after Prometheus)
- Notes: Fluorine is named from Latin "fluere" (to flow), via fluorspar. Cleanly false.

## #10 Neon (Ne-10)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Neon glows bright orange-red when electricity flows through it, making colorful signs.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Pure neon discharge tubes glow red-orange. (Other "neon" sign colors come from other gases or phosphors, but neon itself = red-orange.) Fine for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Neon helps find gold deep underground and start some nuclear reactors.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Helium (closest fit — He-3 in neutron detection used in mineral prospecting; helium as coolant in gas-cooled reactors). Could also reach for Boron (control rods) or Uranium (fuel) for the reactor half.
- Notes: This is a garbled fact that doesn't cleanly belong to any single element. Both halves sound plausible and a child has no easy way to flag it as wrong. The vagueness ("helps find gold") is the mis-teach risk — sounds like real-grown-up science.

## #11 Sodium (Na-11)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Sodium combines with chlorine to make the salt you sprinkle on food.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: NaCl. Perfect kid-level fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Sodium keeps swimming pools clean and is the other half of table salt.
- Verdict: TRUE-PROBLEM
- Belongs to: First half (pool cleaning) belongs to Chlorine; second half ("other half of table salt") IS actually true of sodium.
- Notes: The conjunction is genuinely problematic. A child hearing "Sodium is the other half of table salt" learns a TRUE statement labeled as false. This row should be rewritten — strip the pool-cleaning clause or pick a different distractor entirely. Highest-risk row in the 1-20 range.

## #12 Magnesium (Mg-12)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Magnesium burns with a super-bright white flame, which is why old camera flashes used it.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Mg ribbon burns brilliant white; flashbulbs and flash powder used Mg or Mg/Al. Excellent kid-level fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Magnesium glows bright white in flash bulbs and powers ion engines on space probes.
- Verdict: TRUE-PROBLEM
- Belongs to: First half (white flash bulbs) IS actually true of magnesium. Ion engines = Xenon (most common) or Krypton.
- Notes: Same structural defect as row #11 — the conjunction mixes a true clause with a false one and labels the whole thing false. A child learns "Mg in flash bulbs" as a false statement, contradicting fact_a in the same row. Should be rewritten.

## #13 Aluminum (Al-13)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Aluminum is the light, shiny metal in soda cans and kitchen foil.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Standard, accurate, age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Aluminum's name means it comes before actinium when it breaks down.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Protactinium (Pa-91, literally "before actinium" in its decay chain to actinium)
- Notes: Aluminum is named from Latin "alumen" (alum). Cleanly false; concept is too advanced for a child to mislearn the wrong element anyway.

## #14 Silicon (Si-14)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Silicon is the main ingredient in computer chips and most beach sand.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Silicon chips (Si wafers) and silica (SiO2) sand. Excellent fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Silicon glows bright orange-red when electricity flows through it, making colorful signs.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Neon (recycled verbatim from row #10 fact_a)
- Notes: Silicon is a semiconductor used in LEDs, but it doesn't itself give the orange-red sign glow. Cleanly false.

## #15 Phosphorus (P-15)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Phosphorus is in the tip of every match and helps your bones stay strong.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Red phosphorus on safety-match striker strips; calcium phosphate in bones. Age-appropriate. (Pedantic note: it's the striker strip, not the match head, but fine for a 4yo.)

**fact_b (claimed false):** Phosphorus is the shiniest metal of all and turns black when it sits in the air too long.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Silver (Ag — most reflective metal; tarnishes black from sulfide)
- Notes: Phosphorus is a nonmetal, not a metal at all. White phosphorus does tarnish/discolor in air, so the second clause is *vaguely* plausible-sounding, but the "shiniest metal" framing is clearly wrong.

## #16 Sulfur (S-16)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Sulfur is the bright yellow stuff near volcanoes, and it smells like rotten eggs.
- Verdict: PARTIALLY-TRUE
- Notes: Elemental sulfur deposits near volcanoes — TRUE. Rotten-egg smell — that's actually hydrogen sulfide (H2S), not pure sulfur (which is nearly odorless). Common misconception but acceptable for a 4yo since H2S is sulfur-bearing and the association is universal.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Sulfur was named after Poland by Marie Curie, who discovered it.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Polonium (Po-84, named by Marie Curie after Poland)
- Notes: Sulfur has been known since antiquity; name traces to Sanskrit/Latin. Cleanly false.

## #17 Chlorine (Cl-17)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Chlorine keeps swimming pools clean and is the other half of table salt.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Standard pool sanitizer (as hypochlorite); Cl in NaCl. Age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Chlorine is the lightest metal and powers many phones, tablets, and electric cars.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Lithium (recycled verbatim from row #3 fact_a)
- Notes: Chlorine is a gas/halogen, not a metal. Cleanly false.

## #18 Argon (Ar-18)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Argon is a quiet gas that fills old light bulbs to keep them from burning out.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Argon (and argon-nitrogen mixes) fills incandescent bulbs to slow tungsten filament evaporation. "Quiet gas" = noble/inert is a nice kid framing.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Argon makes fake diamonds that sparkle just like real ones in jewelry.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Zirconium (cubic zirconia, ZrO2 — the standard diamond simulant)
- Notes: Argon is an inert gas and can't form a sparkly crystal. Cleanly false.

## #19 Potassium (K-19)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Bananas are full of potassium, which helps your muscles work properly.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Bananas (~358 mg K per medium banana) and potassium's role in muscle/nerve function. Excellent kid-level fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Potassium is so tough that scientists use it to mark the original meter standard.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Krypton (Kr-86 emission line defined the meter, 1960-1983) — or, for the physical bar, Platinum-Iridium (1889-1960 prototype meter).
- Notes: Potassium is a soft, reactive alkali metal — the opposite of "tough." Most likely the distractor is recycled from a Krypton fact (which is a much closer K symbol/name confuser). Cleanly false; child unlikely to mislearn.

## #20 Calcium (Ca-20)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Calcium builds strong bones and teeth and is in milk, cheese, and yogurt.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Calcium phosphate in bones/teeth; dairy as calcium source. Canonical kid science fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Calcium is named after Georgy Flyorov, a Russian scientist who studied atoms.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Flerovium (Fl-114, named after Georgy Flyorov / the Flerov Lab)
- Notes: Calcium is named from Latin "calx" (lime/chalk). Cleanly false; low mis-teach risk.

## Summary of issues (1-20)

Rows that are NOT cleanly "false statement that belongs to another element":

- **#1 Hydrogen fact_b** — MIS-TEACHABLE. Hydrogen physically WOULD make a balloon float, and the voice-pitch effect would also work; the fact reads as "almost true" rather than clearly false. Plus a child should not learn "balloons can be hydrogen."
- **#10 Neon fact_b** — MIS-TEACHABLE. Garbled fact ("find gold underground / start nuclear reactors") doesn't cleanly belong to any element. Sounds plausible enough to mislearn.
- **#11 Sodium fact_b** — TRUE-PROBLEM. "Is the other half of table salt" IS true of sodium; conjoined with a false clause but the true half stands out and could be mislearned as false.
- **#12 Magnesium fact_b** — TRUE-PROBLEM. "Glows bright white in flash bulbs" IS true of magnesium and directly duplicates fact_a in the same row, labeled false.
- **#15 Phosphorus fact_b** — minor risk: the "turns black in air" clause is vaguely true of white phosphorus, but the "shiniest metal" framing is clearly wrong and dominates.
- **#16 Sulfur fact_a** — PARTIALLY-TRUE. The rotten-egg smell is actually H2S, not pure sulfur. Universal kid-level misconception; probably acceptable.

Rows #11 and #12 are the highest-priority rewrites: both contain a clause that is genuinely true of the named element, labeled "false."

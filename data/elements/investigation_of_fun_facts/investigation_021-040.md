# Investigation: elements 21-40 (Scandium → Zirconium)

## #21 Scandium (Sc-21)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Scandium makes baseball bats and bicycle frames super light and strong.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Aluminum-scandium alloys are real and were famously used in Easton softball/baseball bats and high-end bicycle frames; the claim is accurate and age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Scandium is a metalloid that helps make computer chips and tiny LED lights.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Not cleanly any single element — "metalloid for chips and LEDs" most closely matches silicon (chips) or arsenic/gallium (LED semiconductors). Scandium is a transition metal, NOT a metalloid.
- Notes: The "metalloid for computer chips and tiny LEDs" sentence is repeated verbatim on row #33 (Arsenic) as ITS true fact, so this is a recycled distractor. The category error (calling Sc a metalloid) is the kind of thing a 4yo could absorb as fact.

## #22 Titanium (Ti-22)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Titanium is so strong and light that airplanes, ships, and even toothpaste use it.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Ti metal in aerospace/marine is correct; TiO2 (titanium dioxide) is a common whitener in toothpaste and sunscreen. Slightly conflates the metal with the oxide, but acceptable.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Titanium helps make atomic clocks that keep time more precisely than ever before.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Cesium (the SI second is defined by Cs-133 transitions); rubidium and strontium are also used in atomic clocks. Not titanium.
- Notes: Plausible-sounding because Ti is "high-tech," so it does have mis-teach risk, but it is genuinely not true.

## #23 Vanadium (V-23)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Vanadium makes steel tools extra tough, like the wrenches in a car mechanic's shop.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Chromium-vanadium steel is the standard alloy for wrenches and hand tools — accurate and concrete for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Vanadium is named after Niels Bohr, who imagined how electrons orbit atoms.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Bohrium (element 107) is named after Niels Bohr. Vanadium is named after Vanadis, a Norse goddess (Freyja).
- Notes: Recycled-name distractor; a child has no way to know which element honors Bohr, so this is mildly mis-teachable but the type of fact (etymology) is low-stakes.

## #24 Chromium (Cr-24)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Chromium makes the shiny silver coating on car bumpers and bicycle bells.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Chrome plating is exactly this; iconic and age-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Chromium makes your blood red and is the metal inside skyscrapers and bridges.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Iron (Fe-26) — this is verbatim Iron's fact_a in row #26.
- Notes: Clean recycle from the iron row. Mis-teachable because it sounds authoritative, but it is unambiguously about iron.

## #25 Manganese (Mn-25)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Manganese helps batteries work and gives some glass a pretty purple color.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Mn is in alkaline and Li-ion batteries (cathode chemistry); manganese-violet / "glassmaker's soap" is a real purple glass colorant. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Manganese makes the red color in TV screens and the glow in some banknotes.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Europium — Eu phosphors make red on CRT/plasma TV screens (Y2O3:Eu) and the anti-counterfeit fluorescence on Euro banknotes is europium-based.
- Notes: Strong mis-teach risk because the claim is specific and confident. The red-TV-phosphor fact partly overlaps with Yttrium row #39 ("bright red dots on old TV screens") — yttrium is the host lattice, europium is the activator. For a 4yo this is a real risk of cross-wiring with Yttrium's row.

## #26 Iron (Fe-26)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Iron makes your blood red and is the metal inside skyscrapers and bridges.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Hemoglobin contains iron; structural steel is iron-based. Perfect 4yo-grade fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Iron is named after Enrico Fermi, a scientist who built the first nuclear reactor.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Fermium (element 100) is named after Enrico Fermi. Iron's name comes from Old English "iren"; the symbol Fe is from Latin "ferrum."
- Notes: Etymology distractor, low mis-teach impact.

## #27 Cobalt (Co-27)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Cobalt makes the deep blue color in old pottery and stained-glass windows.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Cobalt blue is the canonical example; correct and concrete.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Bananas are full of Cobalt, which helps your muscles work properly.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Potassium (K-19) — this is verbatim potassium's fact_a (row #19).
- Notes: HIGH mis-teach risk. The "bananas are full of X for muscles" frame is extremely sticky for kids; a 4yo who hears this once will likely conflate cobalt and potassium permanently. Bananas have negligible cobalt. Recommend rewriting this distractor.

## #28 Nickel (Ni-28)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Nickel is the shiny metal in the five-cent coin called a nickel.
- Verdict: PARTIALLY-TRUE
- Notes: The US 5-cent coin IS called a "nickel" but is only 25% nickel (75% copper). Functionally true for a 4yo and the coin shares the name; acceptable but slightly oversimplified.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Nickel makes fireworks burn bright green and helps doctors take pictures of stomachs.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Barium — green fireworks come from barium salts, and barium sulfate is the contrast medium swallowed before stomach/GI X-rays. Both halves point to barium.
- Notes: Mis-teach risk is moderate; combining two real barium facts under "nickel" makes the false claim feel densely true.

## #29 Copper (Cu-29)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Copper turns green when it's outside for a long time, like the Statue of Liberty.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Copper patina (verdigris) on the Statue of Liberty is the textbook example. Perfect.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Copper is named after the planet Neptune, just past Uranus in our solar system.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Neptunium (element 93) is named after Neptune. Copper is named after Cyprus (Latin "cuprum" / "aes cyprium").
- Notes: Etymology distractor. Low mis-teach risk because the actual story (named after the island Cyprus) is a separately fun fact.

## #30 Zinc (Zn-30)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Zinc keeps cuts and scrapes from getting sore and also makes pennies feel light.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Zinc oxide is in diaper-rash/antiseptic creams and modern US pennies are 97.5% zinc with a copper plating (lighter than the old pre-1982 all-copper pennies). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Zinc burns with a super-bright white flame, which is why old camera flashes used it.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Magnesium (Mg-12) — this is verbatim magnesium's fact_a (row #12). Old flashbulbs used magnesium ribbon/wire.
- Notes: Zinc does burn with a bluish-green flame, not the iconic blinding white of magnesium. Mis-teach risk is real but limited (4yo unlikely to test this).

## #31 Gallium (Ga-31)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Gallium melts in your hand because its melting point is below body temperature.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Ga melts at 29.76°C, below the ~37°C body temp; the classic demo. Accurate and memorable.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Gallium makes touchscreens work so you can tap your tablet and phone.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Indium (In-49) — touchscreens use ITO (indium tin oxide). This is verbatim indium's fact_a (row #49).
- Notes: HIGHER risk than typical because gallium IS legitimately in lots of consumer electronics (GaN chargers, GaAs LEDs, GaN in some display drivers), so even an adult might think "yeah, sure." A 4yo would absorb this as fact with no friction. Strong mis-teach.

## #32 Germanium (Ge-32)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Germanium was used in the very first transistors that started the computer age.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: The 1947 Bell Labs point-contact transistor used germanium. Historically accurate and a great kid-fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Germanium is a gas that seeps up from the ground and is checked for in basements.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Radon (Rn-86) — radon is the basement-test gas.
- Notes: Germanium is a solid metalloid, not a gas — the claim is categorically wrong, which makes it less mis-teachable (a child or adult who later learns "germanium is a hard grey solid" will reject this). Reasonable distractor.

## #33 Arsenic (As-33)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Arsenic is a metalloid that helps make computer chips and tiny LED lights.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: As is a metalloid; GaAs (gallium arsenide) is used in high-frequency chips, infrared LEDs, and laser diodes. Accurate but slightly abstract for a 4yo. Note: arsenic is famously poisonous and that context is omitted — for kid-safety the fact is fine (it's about the element's use, not handling).

**fact_b (claimed false):** Arsenic helps make strong glass that doesn't crack when it gets hot.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Boron (B-5) — borosilicate (Pyrex) glass is the heat-resistant glass; verbatim boron's fact_a (row #5).
- Notes: Low mis-teach impact (heat-resistant glass isn't a vivid 4yo concept).

## #34 Selenium (Se-34)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Selenium helps photocopiers see and copy pictures on paper.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Xerographic drums historically used amorphous selenium photoreceptors. Modern copiers often use organic photoconductors instead, but the historical/foundational fact is correct.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Selenium makes steel tools tough enough to cut through other metals.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Molybdenum (Mo-42) — this is verbatim molybdenum's fact_a (row #42). Tungsten and tungsten carbide also fit the "cuts other metals" frame.
- Notes: Recycled cleanly from Mo's row.

## #35 Bromine (Br-35)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Bromine is one of only two liquid elements at room temperature, the other is mercury.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Under standard room conditions, Br and Hg are the only two liquid elements. Accurate. (Cesium, gallium, francium melt just above room temp; this is the standard textbook claim.)

**fact_b (claimed false):** Bromine is so soft you can hammer it into sheets thin enough to see through.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Gold (Au-79) — gold leaf is the iconic hammered-thin-to-translucency element. Also fits silver, aluminum less iconically.
- Notes: Category error (you can't hammer a liquid), which makes it self-defeating as a distractor — a 4yo who connects "bromine is a liquid (fact_a)" to "you hammer bromine into sheets" might find it confusing rather than mis-teachable. Probably fine.

## #36 Krypton (Kr-36)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Krypton glows pale lavender in lights and shares its name with Superman's home planet.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Krypton discharge tubes glow whitish/lavender; Superman's planet is Krypton. Both halves accurate and very kid-friendly.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Krypton is named after Livermore, a California lab where scientists studied it.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Livermorium (element 116) is named after Lawrence Livermore National Lab. Krypton's name is from Greek "kryptos" meaning "hidden."
- Notes: Etymology distractor; safe.

## #37 Rubidium (Rb-37)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Rubidium is named for its ruby-red color when it burns in a flame.
- Verdict: PARTIALLY-TRUE
- Notes: Rubidium is named from Latin "rubidus" (deep red) — but the reference is to its dark-red SPECTRAL LINES seen by Bunsen and Kirchhoff, not its bulk flame color (the Rb flame test is actually a red-violet). For a 4yo "ruby-red when it burns" is close enough and memorable; technically the etymology is about spectral lines.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Rubidium is one of only two liquid elements at room temperature, the other is mercury.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Bromine (Br-35) — this is verbatim bromine's fact_a (row #35).
- Notes: Rb melts at 39.3°C, just above room temp — NEARLY a liquid, which makes this distractor mildly mis-teachable to a careful learner. For a 4yo, fine.

## #38 Strontium (Sr-38)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Strontium makes fireworks burn bright red, which is why holiday rockets are so colorful.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Strontium salts (especially Sr nitrate / Sr carbonate) are THE source of red in fireworks. Iconic and accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Strontium makes super-fast computer chips run cooler and use less power.
- Verdict: AMBIGUOUS
- Belongs to: Not cleanly any element. There are real but obscure uses (SrTiO3 substrates in some advanced electronics, strontium ferrites in magnets, Sr-based ferroelectrics in research memory), but no widely-known "Sr makes chips faster/cooler" fact.
- Notes: This is a fabricated-sounding plausible claim rather than a recycled true-of-another-element fact. Mis-teach risk is HIGH because there is no specific true-of-X anchor to correct it against and it sounds like a confident grown-up tech fact. Recommend rewriting.

## #39 Yttrium (Y-39)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Yttrium helps make the bright red dots on old TV screens.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Y2O3:Eu (europium-doped yttrium oxide) is the red phosphor in CRT TVs. Strictly the activator is europium but yttrium is the host lattice, so the claim holds. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Yttrium is named after Lise Meitner, a scientist who studied splitting atoms.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Meitnerium (element 109) is named after Lise Meitner. Yttrium is named after Ytterby, a village in Sweden (which also named ytterbium, terbium, and erbium).
- Notes: Etymology distractor; safe.

## #40 Zirconium (Zr-40)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Zirconium makes fake diamonds that sparkle just like real ones in jewelry.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Cubic zirconia (ZrO2) is the standard diamond simulant. Accurate and kid-friendly.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Zirconium is the shiny metal in the five-cent coin called a Zirconium.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Nickel (Ni-28) — this is a clumsy rewrite of nickel's fact_a (row #28).
- Notes: The wording is broken ("the five-cent coin called a Zirconium" — there is no such coin name), which makes this distractor self-defeating. A child would likely register confusion rather than learn it as fact, but the broken phrasing is also bad on its own. Recommend rewriting for grammatical sense even if the falsity is obvious to adults.

## Summary of high-mis-teach-risk distractors in this range

The following fact_b entries are flagged as the strongest mis-teach risks for a 4yo and worth prioritizing for rewrite:

- **#27 Cobalt fact_b** — "Bananas are full of Cobalt, which helps your muscles work" recycles potassium's most sticky kid-fact. Highest risk in this range.
- **#31 Gallium fact_b** — "Gallium makes touchscreens work" is mis-teachable because gallium IS legitimately in lots of consumer electronics; a child (or adult) will not feel friction rejecting it.
- **#25 Manganese fact_b** — "red color in TV screens" cross-wires with Yttrium #39's true fact about red TV phosphors.
- **#38 Strontium fact_b** — "Sr makes chips run cooler" is fabricated-sounding rather than recycled; no anchor element to correct against.

Lower-stakes distractors (etymology swaps like #23/#26/#29/#36/#39) are safe — etymology isn't sticky for 4yos and the true names (Vanadis, ferrum, Cyprus, kryptos-hidden, Ytterby) are independently fun facts that can be taught later without conflict.

Two distractors have wording problems independent of factual content:

- **#35 Bromine fact_b** — category error (can't hammer a liquid) makes it confusing rather than mis-teachable.
- **#40 Zirconium fact_b** — "five-cent coin called a Zirconium" is grammatically broken.

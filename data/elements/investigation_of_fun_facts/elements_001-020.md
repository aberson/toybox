# Investigation: Elements 1–20 (Hydrogen → Calcium)

Generated 2026-05-18. Reviewer: agent-1.

## Element 1 — H — Hydrogen
- **fact_a_true:** "Hydrogen is the lightest element and makes up most of the sun."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Hydrogen has atomic number 1 and is the lightest element. The sun is roughly 73% hydrogen by mass and ~92% by atom count — "most of the sun" is accurate at a 4yo level.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Hydrogen makes balloons float and makes voices sound funny when you breathe it in."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes helium)
  - Reason: This is the canonical helium property. Hydrogen IS lighter than air and historically did float balloons (e.g., Hindenburg), so the claim is technically true in isolation but pedagogically misleading — the funny-voice property is uniquely helium. A 4yo will conflate H and He.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 2 — He — Helium
- **fact_a_true:** "Helium makes balloons float and makes voices sound funny when you breathe it in."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Helium is less dense than air (party balloons float) and its lower density raises the speed of sound in the vocal tract, shifting formants upward — the "funny voice" effect.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Helium makes fireworks burn bright red, which is why holiday rockets are so colorful."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes strontium / lithium)
  - Reason: Helium is a noble gas and does not produce firework colors. Red fireworks come from strontium (deep red) or lithium (medium red). Kid risk is moderate — they may incorrectly assign red fireworks to helium.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 3 — Li — Lithium
- **fact_a_true:** "Lithium is the lightest metal and powers many phones, tablets, and electric cars."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Lithium is the least dense solid metal (0.534 g/cm³) and the dominant chemistry in modern rechargeable batteries. "Lightest metal" can be misread as "lightest element" but the wording is precise.
  - Concern: LOW
- **fact_b_false:** "Lithium helps jet engines spin hot and fast without melting."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes rhenium / nickel superalloys)
  - Reason: Lithium has a very low melting point (~180 °C) and is unsuitable for jet engines. High-temperature jet alloys use rhenium, nickel, and cobalt. Kid risk is low — the fact is abstract.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 4 — Be — Beryllium
- **fact_a_true:** "Beryllium is a strong, light metal that helps build airplanes and spaceships."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Beryllium is low density (1.85 g/cm³), stiff, and used in aerospace structures (e.g., JWST mirrors, satellite parts, missile/aircraft components).
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Beryllium is named after Nicolaus Copernicus, who taught that Earth circles the sun."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes copernicium, element 112)
  - Reason: Beryllium is named after the mineral beryl (Greek beryllos). Copernicium (Cn, 112) is the Copernicus-named element. Low concern because Copernicus is unknown to a 4yo.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 5 — B — Boron
- **fact_a_true:** "Boron helps make strong glass that doesn't crack when it gets hot."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Borosilicate glass (Pyrex, lab glass) uses boron oxide to give it low thermal expansion so it resists thermal shock.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Boron melts in your hand because its melting point is below body temperature."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes gallium; also true of cesium/rubidium near body temp)
  - Reason: Boron's melting point is ~2076 °C — it is one of the highest-melting elements. The melt-in-hand property is gallium (29.8 °C). Kid risk is moderate because the image is memorable.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 6 — C — Carbon
- **fact_a_true:** "Carbon is in every living thing on Earth, from trees to people to your pet dog."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: All known life is carbon-based; carbon forms the backbone of organic molecules (proteins, DNA, sugars, fats).
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Carbon colors glass and ceramics a pretty pink and helps light travel through cables."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes erbium)
  - Reason: Pink glass/ceramic colorant and erbium-doped fiber-optic amplifiers are both erbium properties. Carbon in fibers usually means soot/black, not pink. Low concern because "pink glass" is unlikely to compete with carbon's strong life-association in a kid's mind.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 7 — N — Nitrogen
- **fact_a_true:** "Most of the air we breathe is nitrogen, even more than oxygen."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Earth's atmosphere is ~78% N₂ and ~21% O₂ by volume. Counter-intuitive for kids (they think air = oxygen) but accurate.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Nitrogen is named after Dubna, a Russian town where scientists made it."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes dubnium, element 105)
  - Reason: Nitrogen is named from Greek nitron (soda/saltpeter) + genes (forming). Dubnium (Db, 105) is the Dubna-named element. Low concern because Dubna means nothing to a 4yo.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 8 — O — Oxygen
- **fact_a_true:** "Oxygen is the part of the air our lungs use to keep us alive."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Hemoglobin in red blood cells binds O₂ for aerobic respiration. Accurate kid-level framing.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Oxygen makes the shiny silver coating on car bumpers and bicycle bells."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes chromium)
  - Reason: Chrome plating is chromium. Oxygen is involved in oxidation/tarnish, which is the opposite phenomenon. Low concern — the fact will read as random.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 9 — F — Fluorine
- **fact_a_true:** "Fluorine helps make toothpaste that keeps your teeth strong."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Fluoride compounds (sodium fluoride, stannous fluoride) in toothpaste strengthen tooth enamel and reduce decay. Kid-level conflation of "fluorine" (element) with "fluoride" (ion) is the common usage.
  - Concern: LOW
- **fact_b_false:** "Fluorine is named after a hero from Greek myths who gave fire to people."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes promethium, element 61)
  - Reason: Fluorine is named from Latin fluere ("to flow") via fluorspar. Prometheus is the promethium namesake. Low concern at age 4.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 10 — Ne — Neon
- **fact_a_true:** "Neon glows bright orange-red when electricity flows through it, making colorful signs."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Neon discharge tubes emit the characteristic red-orange glow (~640 nm). Other "neon" sign colors actually use other noble gases or phosphors, but pure neon = red-orange is correct.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Neon helps find gold deep underground and start some nuclear reactors."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes californium, element 98)
  - Reason: Californium-252 is a neutron source used for gold/oil-well logging and reactor startup. Neon is an inert noble gas with neither use. Low concern — fact is abstract.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 11 — Na — Sodium
- **fact_a_true:** "Sodium combines with chlorine to make the salt you sprinkle on food."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: NaCl (sodium chloride) is table salt — sodium and chlorine ionically bonded.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Sodium keeps swimming pools clean and is the other half of table salt."
  - Verdict: MISLEADING (chlorine is the pool sanitizer; sodium IS half of table salt)
  - Reason: The second clause is TRUE — sodium IS one half of NaCl. The first clause (pool cleaning) describes chlorine. Because half the statement is correct, a 4yo could lock in "sodium cleans pools" alongside the salt fact, which is wrong. Mixed-truth distractors are the highest mislearning risk.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 12 — Mg — Magnesium
- **fact_a_true:** "Magnesium burns with a super-bright white flame, which is why old camera flashes used it."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Magnesium ribbon burns at ~3,100 °C with brilliant white light. Early flash photography used magnesium powder/flash bulbs.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Magnesium glows bright white in flash bulbs and powers ion engines on space probes."
  - Verdict: MISLEADING (first half is actually correct for Mg; second half is xenon)
  - Reason: Magnesium DID power old flash bulbs (first clause is true). Ion engines (Dawn, Hayabusa, etc.) use xenon, not magnesium. This is a mixed-truth distractor that will reinforce a true Mg fact while planting a false xenon-as-magnesium belief.
  - Concern: HIGH

## Element 13 — Al — Aluminum
- **fact_a_true:** "Aluminum is the light, shiny metal in soda cans and kitchen foil."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Soda cans and household foil are aluminum — light, malleable, corrosion-resistant.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Aluminum's name means it comes before actinium when it breaks down."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes protactinium, element 91)
  - Reason: Aluminum is named from alumen (Latin for alum). Protactinium ("before actinium") decays into actinium. Low concern at age 4 — the etymology is too abstract to stick.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 14 — Si — Silicon
- **fact_a_true:** "Silicon is the main ingredient in computer chips and most beach sand."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Beach sand is largely silicon dioxide (quartz); semiconductor wafers are purified elemental silicon.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Silicon glows bright orange-red when electricity flows through it, making colorful signs."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes neon)
  - Reason: This is the neon-sign property. Silicon is a semiconductor; some Si LEDs emit red but the gas-discharge "glowing sign" description belongs to neon. Concern is low — kids strongly associate silicon with chips, not signs.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 15 — P — Phosphorus
- **fact_a_true:** "Phosphorus is in the tip of every match and helps your bones stay strong."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Strike-anywhere matches historically used white phosphorus; modern safety matches use red phosphorus on the striker strip. Phosphorus is also a major bone-mineral component (calcium phosphate in hydroxyapatite).
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Phosphorus is the shiniest metal of all and turns black when it sits in the air too long."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes silver)
  - Reason: Phosphorus is a non-metal. White phosphorus does ignite/oxidize in air (turning yellowish), and red phosphorus is dull. Silver is the most-reflective metal that tarnishes black via Ag₂S. Concern moderate because phosphorus actually IS air-sensitive, which could anchor the false belief.
  - Concern: MED

## Element 16 — S — Sulfur
- **fact_a_true:** "Sulfur is the bright yellow stuff near volcanoes, and it smells like rotten eggs."
  - Verdict: MISLEADING (sulfur itself is odorless; the rotten-egg smell is H₂S)
  - Reason: Yellow volcanic sulfur deposits — TRUE. The smell — kid-level shorthand. Pure elemental sulfur is essentially odorless; the rotten-egg smell is hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), a compound of sulfur. This is a near-universal kid-level simplification and is acceptable for a 4yo curriculum.
  - Concern: LOW
- **fact_b_false:** "Sulfur was named after Poland by Marie Curie, who discovered it."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes polonium, element 84)
  - Reason: Sulfur is one of the elements known since antiquity (Sanskrit sulvere → Latin sulphur). Polonium is the Curie/Poland element. Low concern because the kid doesn't know Poland or Marie Curie yet.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 17 — Cl — Chlorine
- **fact_a_true:** "Chlorine keeps swimming pools clean and is the other half of table salt."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Both clauses correct — chlorine (as hypochlorite) sanitizes pools, and Cl is one half of NaCl.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Chlorine is the lightest metal and powers many phones, tablets, and electric cars."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes lithium)
  - Reason: Chlorine is a non-metal halogen gas. Lithium is the lightest metal and the battery chemistry. Low concern — "lightest metal" doesn't fit kid's mental image of chlorine (pool smell).
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 18 — Ar — Argon
- **fact_a_true:** "Argon is a quiet gas that fills old light bulbs to keep them from burning out."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Argon (and argon/nitrogen mixes) fills incandescent bulbs to prevent tungsten filament oxidation/sublimation. "Quiet" is a kid-friendly gloss on "inert."
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Argon makes fake diamonds that sparkle just like real ones in jewelry."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes zirconium / cubic zirconia)
  - Reason: Cubic zirconia (ZrO₂) is the standard diamond simulant. Argon is a noble gas and forms no jewelry. Low concern.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 19 — K — Potassium
- **fact_a_true:** "Bananas are full of potassium, which helps your muscles work properly."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Bananas are a well-known dietary K source (~422 mg per medium banana). Potassium ions are required for muscle contraction and nerve impulse transmission.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Potassium is so tough that scientists use it to mark the original meter standard."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes the platinum-iridium International Prototype Metre, primarily iridium)
  - Reason: The original meter prototype was a platinum-iridium alloy bar (90% Pt, 10% Ir). Potassium is a soft, reactive alkali metal (cuts with a knife). Low concern — abstract claim.
  - Concern: LOW

## Element 20 — Ca — Calcium
- **fact_a_true:** "Calcium builds strong bones and teeth and is in milk, cheese, and yogurt."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Calcium phosphate (hydroxyapatite) is the mineral matrix of bones and teeth; dairy is the canonical dietary calcium source.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Calcium is named after Georgy Flyorov, a Russian scientist who studied atoms."
  - Verdict: FALSE (distractor describes flerovium, element 114)
  - Reason: Calcium is named from Latin calx (lime/limestone). Flerovium honors Georgy Flyorov. Low concern at age 4.
  - Concern: LOW

# Investigation: elements 81-100 (Thallium → Fermium)

## #81 Thallium (Tl-81)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Thallium is one of the few elements whose name comes from a Greek word for a green twig.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: From Greek "thallos" (green shoot/twig); Crookes named it after the bright green spectral line he saw. Fine for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Thallium combines with chlorine to make the salt you sprinkle on food.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Sodium (the "salt you sprinkle on food" is NaCl, sodium chloride)
- Notes: TlCl does exist as a real (highly toxic) compound, so phrasing "thallium combines with chlorine to make the salt" is technically partly true at a chemistry level even though the table-salt framing is wrong. A 4yo could plausibly conclude thallium is edible salt — risky given thallium's real-world toxicity.

## #82 Lead (Pb-82)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Lead is one of the heaviest common metals and was used in pencils long ago.
- Verdict: MISLEADING
- Notes: Lead is indeed a heavy common metal, but pencil "lead" has ALWAYS been graphite (carbon), never elemental lead — this is the textbook misconception. Teaching it as true to a 4yo cements the very confusion most science education works to undo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Lead paints used to make sunny yellow colors in old artists' paintings.
- Verdict: TRUE-PROBLEM
- Belongs to: Lead (lead chromate / "chrome yellow", lead-tin yellow, Naples yellow are all historic lead-based yellow pigments)
- Notes: This is actually TRUE about lead. Lead-based yellow pigments (lead-tin yellow, Naples yellow / lead antimonate, chrome yellow / lead chromate) were widely used by old masters. Should not be used as a distractor.

## #83 Bismuth (Bi-83)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Bismuth grows into beautiful rainbow staircase crystals that look like tiny castles.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Bismuth's hopper crystals with iridescent oxide layers are a well-known visual — accurate and a good 4yo description.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Bismuth is so rare that less than a teaspoonful exists on the whole planet.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Astatine (Astatine — the rarest naturally occurring element, total natural amount estimated under 1 gram)
- Notes: Bismuth is actually fairly abundant and used in Pepto-Bismol, fire-suppression sprinklers, etc. Clean recycled fact.

## #84 Polonium (Po-84)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Polonium was named after Poland by Marie Curie, who discovered it.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Marie Curie named it for her homeland Poland in 1898. Fine for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Polonium is a strong, light metal that helps build airplanes and spaceships.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Beryllium (or possibly aluminum/titanium, but matches the entry #4 Beryllium fact verbatim)
- Notes: Polonium is a highly radioactive metalloid, not a structural metal. Clean.

## #85 Astatine (At-85)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Astatine is so rare that less than a teaspoonful exists on the whole planet.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Estimated <1 gram naturally occurring in Earth's crust at any time. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Astatine helps make super-fast magnets that lift trains off the tracks.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Neodymium (maglev trains use Nd-Fe-B magnets)
- Notes: Astatine is too rare and radioactive for any structural use. Clean.

## #86 Radon (Rn-86)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Radon is a gas that seeps up from the ground and is checked for in basements.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Accurate and age-appropriate; matches common home-safety messaging.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Radon is so rare that even less of it exists than gold, and it makes fancy wedding rings.
- Verdict: AMBIGUOUS
- Belongs to: Rhodium (wedding-ring plating); the rarer-than-gold claim is also true of rhodium and several others.
- Notes: Radon IS rarer than gold in crustal abundance (so part of the claim is accidentally TRUE), but radon is a radioactive noble gas and absolutely not used in wedding rings. The compound sentence is half-true / half-absurd; a 4yo might just absorb "radon is in wedding rings," which is dangerous-adjacent (radon = lung-cancer risk). Recommend rewriting.

## #87 Francium (Fr-87)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Francium is so rare that there are only a few atoms of it on Earth at any time.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Estimated total natural inventory ~20-30 grams at any moment; "a few atoms" is an under-estimate but in spirit correct for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Francium is named after Moscow, the capital city of Russia.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Moscovium (Mc-115)
- Notes: Francium is named after France (discovered by Marguerite Perey at the Curie Institute, Paris, 1939). Clean.

## #88 Radium (Ra-88)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Radium glows in the dark and was once painted on old watch dials to read the time.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Accurate (Radium Girls era, ~1917-1930s). Good 4yo fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Radium helps photocopiers see and copy pictures on paper.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Selenium (photoconductive drums in xerography)
- Notes: Radium has no role in photocopying. Clean.

## #89 Actinium (Ac-89)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Actinium glows a pretty pale blue in the dark and starts the actinide family of elements.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Actinium glows pale blue due to ionization of surrounding air from its intense alpha radioactivity; it is the namesake of the actinide series. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Actinium is one of the rarest and most expensive metals to dig out of the ground.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Not a clean match — rhodium is the priciest "dug" metal; actinium itself is in fact extremely rare and extremely expensive, but it's produced in reactors not mined.
- Notes: Actinium-227 sells for ~tens of millions of dollars per gram, so "rarest and most expensive" is arguably TRUE in spirit; the falsehood hinges only on "dig out of the ground" (it's produced by neutron irradiation, not mined). A 4yo would not catch that distinction. Recommend rewriting.

## #90 Thorium (Th-90)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Thorium is named after Thor, the Norse god of thunder.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named by Berzelius after the Norse god Thor in 1828. Fine for 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Thorium is named after the German state of Hesse, where scientists made it.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Hassium (Hs-108, named after Hesse / Latin "Hassia")
- Notes: Clean recycled fact.

## #91 Protactinium (Pa-91)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Protactinium's name means it comes before actinium when it breaks down.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: "Proto-actinium" — Pa-231 alpha-decays into Ac-227. Accurate and a nice etymology.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Protactinium makes the deep blue color in old pottery and stained-glass windows.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Cobalt (cobalt blue in pottery and stained glass)
- Notes: Clean.

## #92 Uranium (U-92)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Uranium is named after the planet Uranus and powers nuclear reactors that make electricity.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named by Klaproth in 1789 after the newly discovered planet Uranus; reactor fuel — accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Uranium helps doctors take clearer pictures inside the body with MRI scans.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Gadolinium (MRI contrast agents)
- Notes: Uranium has no medical-imaging role. Clean.

## #93 Neptunium (Np-93)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Neptunium is named after the planet Neptune, just past Uranus in our solar system.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named in 1940 by McMillan & Abelson because it follows uranium just as Neptune follows Uranus. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Neptunium is named after the scientist who first organized the periodic table.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Mendelevium (Md-101, named after Mendeleev)
- Notes: Clean.

## #94 Plutonium (Pu-94)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Plutonium is named after the dwarf planet Pluto and powers some space probes.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named for Pluto (then still a planet) following the Uranus/Neptune/Pluto sequence; Pu-238 RTGs power Voyager, Cassini, Curiosity, Perseverance. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Plutonium builds strong bones and teeth and is in milk, cheese, and yogurt.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Calcium
- Notes: Plutonium is extremely toxic and definitively NOT in food. Clean — though ironically the contrast makes this a good "obviously wrong" distractor for an older kid.

## #95 Americium (Am-95)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Americium is in many home smoke detectors and helps keep families safe from fires.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Am-241 alpha source in ionization-type smoke detectors. Accurate and a great 4yo fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Americium makes camera lenses sharper so pictures look crisp and clear.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Lanthanum (lanthanum oxide in high-refractive-index camera lens glass)
- Notes: Clean.

## #96 Curium (Cm-96)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Curium is named after Marie and Pierre Curie, two famous element-discovering scientists.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named in 1944 by Seaborg's team after the Curies. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Curium is named after the dwarf planet Pluto and powers some space probes.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Plutonium (verbatim match to #94 fact_a)
- Notes: Clean recycled fact.

## #97 Berkelium (Bk-97)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Berkelium is named after Berkeley, California, where scientists first made it.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Synthesized at UC Berkeley in 1949. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Berkelium is the light, shiny metal in soda cans and kitchen foil.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Aluminum
- Notes: Clean.

## #98 Californium (Cf-98)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Californium helps find gold deep underground and start some nuclear reactors.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Cf-252 is used as a portable neutron source in mineral/oil well logging (including precious-metal prospecting) and as a neutron source for nuclear reactor startup. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Californium coats steel cans so the soup or beans inside don't rust the can.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Tin (tin-plated steel "tin cans")
- Notes: Clean.

## #99 Einsteinium (Es-99)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Einsteinium is named after Albert Einstein, the famous scientist who imagined time bending.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named after Einstein after being discovered in the 1952 "Ivy Mike" thermonuclear test fallout. "Imagined time bending" is a reasonable 4yo handle on relativity.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Einsteinium is so rare that there are only a few atoms of it on Earth at any time.
- Verdict: AMBIGUOUS
- Belongs to: Francium (verbatim match to #87 fact_a)
- Notes: Einsteinium IS extremely rare — total accumulated production is on the order of milligrams, and natural occurrence is essentially zero. The "few atoms on Earth" framing is much closer to francium's situation, but a knowledgeable 4yo (or parent) could be forgiven for thinking it applies to einsteinium too. Mostly correctly false, but soft.

## #100 Fermium (Fm-100)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Fermium is named after Enrico Fermi, a scientist who built the first nuclear reactor.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Named after Fermi who designed and ran Chicago Pile-1 (the first artificial nuclear reactor, 1942). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Fermium is in every living thing on Earth, from trees to people to your pet dog.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Carbon (or oxygen/hydrogen/nitrogen — but carbon is the canonical "in every living thing")
- Notes: Clean.

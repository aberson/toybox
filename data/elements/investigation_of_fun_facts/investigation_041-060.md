# Investigation: elements 41-60 (Niobium → Neodymium)

## #41 Niobium (Nb-41)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Niobium helps make super-fast magnets that lift trains off the tracks.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Niobium-titanium and niobium-tin are the standard superconducting magnet alloys used in maglev trains, MRI machines, and particle accelerators. Kid-appropriate framing is fine.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Niobium makes the bright green color you see on TV and tablet screens.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Terbium (Tb-65) is the canonical green phosphor in displays; europium gives red, terbium green. Niobium is not used for screen color.
- Notes: A child might mislearn "Nb = green screens" but the claim is verifiably wrong; safe to keep as a distractor.

## #42 Molybdenum (Mo-42)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Molybdenum makes steel tools tough enough to cut through other metals.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Molybdenum is a core hardening element in high-speed steel (M2, M42) used for drill bits and cutting tools. Accurate and kid-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Molybdenum is named after Alfred Nobel, who started the Nobel Prizes.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Nobelium (No-102) is named after Alfred Nobel. Molybdenum is named from Greek "molybdos" meaning lead, because the ore was confused with lead ore.
- Notes: Clean distractor — the Nobel attribution belongs unambiguously to a different element.

## #43 Technetium (Tc-43)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Technetium was the first element ever made by people, in a lab in 1937.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Synthesized by Perrier and Segrè in 1937 in Palermo from molybdenum bombarded with deuterons — the first artificially produced element. Kid-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Technetium makes baseball bats and bicycle frames super light and strong.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Scandium (Sc-21) — Sc-Al alloys are used in premium baseball bats and bicycle frames.
- Notes: Technetium is radioactive and never used in sporting goods; the claim is obviously wrong on inspection.

## #44 Ruthenium (Ru-44)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Ruthenium makes the tiny gold tip on fancy fountain pens last extra long.
- Verdict: PARTIALLY-TRUE
- Notes: The canonical "iridium-tipped" fountain pen alloys historically use Os/Ir; modern nib tips often use Ru-containing platinum-group alloys. Ruthenium IS a fountain-pen nib alloy component, but iridium and osmium are more famous. Defensible but not the textbook example.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Ruthenium polishes glass to a mirror finish and is the most common rare-earth metal.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Cerium (Ce-58) — cerium oxide (jeweler's rouge / "opaline") is THE glass polish, and cerium is the most abundant rare-earth element. Ruthenium isn't even a rare earth.
- Notes: Clean swap with Cerium's true fact (which appears verbatim at row #58 fact_a).

## #45 Rhodium (Rh-45)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Rhodium is one of the rarest metals and gives jewelry its bright silvery shine.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Rhodium is among the rarest stable metals in Earth's crust and is electroplated onto white-gold jewelry for its bright reflective finish. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Rhodium is in many home smoke detectors and helps keep families safe from fires.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Americium (Am-95) — Am-241 is the radioactive isotope used in ionization smoke detectors.
- Notes: Clean distractor; rhodium has nothing to do with smoke detectors.

## #46 Palladium (Pd-46)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Palladium cleans up car exhaust so the air stays cleaner to breathe.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Palladium (along with platinum and rhodium) is a key catalyst in automotive catalytic converters. Accurate and kid-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Palladium is the main ingredient in computer chips and most beach sand.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Silicon (Si-14) — that's silicon's fact verbatim from row #14.
- Notes: Clean swap distractor.

## #47 Silver (Ag-47)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Silver is the shiniest metal of all and turns black when it sits in the air too long.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Silver has the highest optical reflectivity of any metal in the visible spectrum and tarnishes to black silver sulfide from atmospheric sulfur compounds. Both halves accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Silver helps make fire-resistant clothes that don't catch fire easily.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Antimony (Sb-51) — antimony trioxide is a common flame-retardant synergist used in textiles. (Bromine compounds and aluminum hydroxide are also used.)
- Notes: Note that this fact appears as fact_a for Sb at row #51, so it's a clean recycled-truth distractor.

## #48 Cadmium (Cd-48)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Cadmium paints used to make sunny yellow colors in old artists' paintings.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Cadmium sulfide ("cadmium yellow") was a staple of impressionist and post-impressionist palettes; van Gogh's sunflowers are a famous example. Accurate; the past-tense framing is good because of toxicity.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Cadmium glows in the dark and was once painted on old watch dials to read the time.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Radium (Ra-88) — radium-based luminous paint on watch dials is the famous case (and the "Radium Girls" history).
- Notes: Cadmium does not phosphoresce; the distractor is unambiguous.

## #49 Indium (In-49)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Indium makes touchscreens work so you can tap your tablet and phone.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Indium tin oxide (ITO) is the transparent conductor used in nearly all capacitive touchscreens. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Most of the air we breathe is Indium, even more than oxygen.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Nitrogen (N-7) — that's nitrogen's fact verbatim from row #7.
- Notes: Obviously absurd; safe distractor.

## #50 Tin (Sn-50)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Tin coats steel cans so the soup or beans inside don't rust the can.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: "Tin cans" are steel cans electroplated with a thin tin layer to prevent rust and prevent iron from contaminating the food. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Tin makes the tiny gold tip on fancy fountain pens last extra long.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Ruthenium / Iridium / Osmium — the platinum-group "iridium-tipped" nib alloys. (Mirrors row #44 fact_a.)
- Notes: Clean distractor.

## #51 Antimony (Sb-51)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Antimony helps make fire-resistant clothes that don't catch fire easily.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Antimony trioxide is widely used as a flame-retardant synergist in textiles and plastics. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Antimony is one of the few elements whose name comes from a Greek word for a green twig.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly), but MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Not true of any element. Praseodymium (Pr-59) means "green twin" (Greek "prasios didymos"), not "green twig" — so this fact is a slightly garbled echo of Pr's etymology. Antimony's real etymology is debated (commonly Greek "anti monos" = "not alone," or from Arabic "ithmid").
- Notes: Risky because it sounds plausibly etymological and a child wouldn't have any way to know it's wrong. Consider replacing — no real element is "named after a green twig," so the kid can't even correct the mislearning by re-association.

## #52 Tellurium (Te-52)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Tellurium helps solar panels turn sunshine into power for homes.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Cadmium telluride (CdTe) is the second-most-deployed thin-film photovoltaic technology after silicon (First Solar's flagship product). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Tellurium is the heaviest element ever made and is named after Yuri Oganessian.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Oganesson (Og-118) — element 118, the heaviest synthesized element, named after Yuri Oganessian in 2016.
- Notes: Clean distractor; tellurium is named after Latin "tellus" (earth).

## #53 Iodine (I-53)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Iodine is added to table salt to help your body grow strong.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Iodized salt prevents iodine deficiency / goiter and supports thyroid hormone production. "Grow strong" is loose but acceptable for a 4yo.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Iodine is named for its ruby-red color when it burns in a flame.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Rubidium (Rb-37) — "rubidium" is from Latin "rubidus" (deep red) for its flame-test color. (Mirrors row #37 fact_a.)
- Notes: Iodine IS named for its color, but the color is violet (Greek "iodes" = violet-colored), not red. The half-truth that iodine's name is color-derived might make this stick — but the specific "ruby-red" claim is unambiguously wrong.

## #54 Xenon (Xe-54)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Xenon glows bright white in flash bulbs and powers ion engines on space probes.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Xenon flash lamps are standard in camera/strobe flashes; xenon ion thrusters powered Deep Space 1, Dawn, BepiColombo, and Starlink. Both halves accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Xenon has the highest melting point of any metal, which is why it's in light-bulb wires.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Tungsten (W-74) — tungsten has the highest melting point of any metal (3422 C) and is the incandescent-bulb filament material.
- Notes: Xenon is a noble gas, not a metal — the claim is internally inconsistent and safe to keep as a distractor.

## #55 Cesium (Cs-55)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Cesium runs the most accurate clocks in the world, losing only a second every millions of years.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: The SI second is defined by the cesium-133 hyperfine transition. Cesium fountain clocks lose ~1 second per 100+ million years. "Millions of years" is accurate (conservative).

**fact_b (claimed false):** Cesium is named after Wilhelm Roentgen, who discovered X-rays.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Roentgenium (Rg-111) — named after Wilhelm Röntgen.
- Notes: Cesium is from Latin "caesius" (sky blue) for its spectral lines. Clean distractor.

## #56 Barium (Ba-56)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Barium makes fireworks burn bright green and helps doctors take pictures of stomachs.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Barium salts are the standard green-firework colorant; barium sulfate is the X-ray contrast agent for upper-GI imaging. Both halves accurate and kid-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Barium keeps cuts and scrapes from getting sore and also makes pennies feel light.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Zinc (Zn-30) — zinc oxide is in diaper-rash and antiseptic creams, and post-1982 US pennies are 97.5% zinc with copper plating. (Mirrors row #30 fact_a.)
- Notes: Clean recycled-truth distractor.

## #57 Lanthanum (La-57)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Lanthanum makes camera lenses sharper so pictures look crisp and clear.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Lanthanum oxide is added to high-refractive-index optical glass used in premium camera lenses (especially Canon's L-series and other low-dispersion designs). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Lanthanum creates the strongest magnetic fields of any natural element.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Neodymium (Nd-60) — NdFeB magnets are the strongest commercial permanent magnets. (Adjacent to row #60 fact_a.)
- Notes: Slightly MIS-TEACHABLE because lanthanide-series elements are vaguely magnetic-sounding, and a child might generalize "lanthanum = magnets." Holmium technically has the highest atomic magnetic moment, so the superlative is loose, but the spirit of the answer is Nd.

## #58 Cerium (Ce-58)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Cerium polishes glass to a mirror finish and is the most common rare-earth metal.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Cerium oxide ("opaline" / jeweler's rouge) is the industry-standard glass polish; cerium is the most abundant rare-earth element in Earth's crust (~66 ppm, more abundant than copper). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Cerium is named after Ernest Rutherford, who discovered the center of the atom.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Rutherfordium (Rf-104) — named after Ernest Rutherford.
- Notes: Cerium is named after the dwarf planet Ceres (discovered 1801, same year as the element). Clean distractor.

## #59 Praseodymium (Pr-59)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Praseodymium colors special goggles green so welders can see safely through bright sparks.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Didymium glass (Pr + Nd mix) is used in welder's and glassblower's goggles to block the bright yellow sodium-D line; the glass has a characteristic green tint from Pr. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Praseodymium makes steel tools extra tough, like the wrenches in a car mechanic's shop.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Vanadium (V-23) — that's vanadium's true fact verbatim from row #23.
- Notes: Clean recycled-truth distractor.

## #60 Neodymium (Nd-60)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Neodymium makes the strongest magnets on Earth, found in headphones and hard drives.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: NdFeB (Nd2Fe14B) magnets are the strongest commercially available permanent magnets and are ubiquitous in headphones, HDD voice-coil motors, EV traction motors, and wind turbines. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Neodymium is named after a hero from Greek myths who gave fire to people.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Promethium (Pm-61) — named after Prometheus, the Titan who gave fire to humanity. (Appears as Pm's fact_a at row #61.)
- Notes: Neodymium is named from Greek "neos didymos" (new twin). Clean recycled-truth distractor.

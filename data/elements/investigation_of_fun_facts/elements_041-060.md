# Investigation: Elements 41-60 (Niobium -> Neodymium)

Generated 2026-05-18. Reviewer: agent-3.

Convention: when `fact_b_false` is actually TRUE about a DIFFERENT element, that's flagged as a "mis-attribution" — pedagogically the worst kind because it sounds plausible. The kid might mislearn which element does what.

## Element 41 — Nb — Niobium
- **fact_a_true:** "Niobium helps make super-fast magnets that lift trains off the tracks."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Nb-Ti superconducting alloy is the working material in maglev train coils (e.g. Japan's L0 SCMAGLEV cooled by liquid helium) and in MRI magnets.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Niobium makes the bright green color you see on TV and tablet screens."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Terbium)
  - Reason: The green phosphor in CRTs/LCD-backlight phosphors is terbium-doped (Tb3+); niobium has no role as a display green phosphor.
  - Concern: HIGH — sounds plausible and is the truth about Tb (row 65); the kid could swap the two.

## Element 42 — Mo — Molybdenum
- **fact_a_true:** "Molybdenum makes steel tools tough enough to cut through other metals."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Mo is a classic alloying element in high-speed steel and tool steels (e.g. M2 steel) — increases hardness and high-temperature strength.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Molybdenum is named after Alfred Nobel, who started the Nobel Prizes."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Nobelium)
  - Reason: Mo's name comes from Greek "molybdos" (lead) — it was confused with lead ores. Nobelium (No, 102) is the one named for Alfred Nobel.
  - Concern: MED — plausible-sounding to a 4yo who can't tell the names apart; could mislearn the Nobelium fact.

## Element 43 — Tc — Technetium
- **fact_a_true:** "Technetium was the first element ever made by people, in a lab in 1937."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Perrier and Segre synthesized Tc in 1937 in Palermo by deuteron-bombarding molybdenum — first artificially produced element.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Technetium makes baseball bats and bicycle frames super light and strong."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Scandium)
  - Reason: Sc-Al alloy is used for high-end baseball bats and bicycle frames. Tc is radioactive and not used in sporting goods.
  - Concern: MED — sounds plausible (sporty), but Tc is so unfamiliar to the kid that confusion risk is lower than the display-phosphor ones.

## Element 44 — Ru — Ruthenium
- **fact_a_true:** "Ruthenium makes the tiny gold tip on fancy fountain pens last extra long."
  - Verdict: TRUE (slightly imprecise)
  - Reason: Fountain-pen "iridium tipping" is in practice an alloy of platinum-group metals — most commonly Os/Ru/Ir — because pure iridium is no longer used. Ruthenium is a routine ingredient. The "gold tip" framing is a small simplification (the gold nib has a hard PGM ball welded to it), but defensible for a 4yo.
  - Concern: LOW — minor simplification, not factually wrong.
- **fact_b_false:** "Ruthenium polishes glass to a mirror finish and is the most common rare-earth metal."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Cerium)
  - Reason: Cerium oxide is the classic glass-polishing compound, and Ce is the most abundant rare-earth metal. Ru is a rare platinum-group metal, not a rare earth, and is uncommon.
  - Concern: HIGH — directly the row-58 Cerium fact verbatim; a kid who hears both could pick the wrong attribution.

## Element 45 — Rh — Rhodium
- **fact_a_true:** "Rhodium is one of the rarest metals and gives jewelry its bright silvery shine."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Rhodium plating is the standard finish on white-gold jewelry; Rh is one of the rarest stable metals in the crust.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Rhodium is in many home smoke detectors and helps keep families safe from fires."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Americium)
  - Reason: Americium-241 (row 95) is the radioactive source in ionization smoke detectors. Rhodium has no smoke-detector role.
  - Concern: HIGH — exact match to the Americium row, classic mis-attribution.

## Element 46 — Pd — Palladium
- **fact_a_true:** "Palladium cleans up car exhaust so the air stays cleaner to breathe."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Pd is one of the three platinum-group metals (with Pt and Rh) used in automotive catalytic converters, especially for gasoline engines.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Palladium is the main ingredient in computer chips and most beach sand."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Silicon)
  - Reason: Silicon (row 14) is the chip / beach-sand element. Pd is a precious metal, not used as the chip substrate.
  - Concern: HIGH — flagged explicitly by the prompt; plausible-sounding to a kid who's learning about chips.

## Element 47 — Ag — Silver
- **fact_a_true:** "Silver is the shiniest metal of all and turns black when it sits in the air too long."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Silver has the highest optical reflectivity of any metal; it tarnishes to black Ag2S in air with H2S.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Silver helps make fire-resistant clothes that don't catch fire easily."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Antimony)
  - Reason: Antimony trioxide (Sb2O3) is the standard flame retardant in fabrics, plastics, and electronics. Silver is used as an antimicrobial textile additive, not a flame retardant.
  - Concern: MED — silver IS in some technical fabrics (just for a different reason), so the claim is sneakily close to a real fact.

## Element 48 — Cd — Cadmium
- **fact_a_true:** "Cadmium paints used to make sunny yellow colors in old artists' paintings."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Cadmium yellow (CdS) and cadmium red (CdSe-CdS) were standard artist pigments from the late 19th century — Van Gogh's sunflowers famously contain cadmium yellow.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Cadmium glows in the dark and was once painted on old watch dials to read the time."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Radium)
  - Reason: Radium (row 88) is the watch-dial element — radium dial paint caused the famous "Radium Girls" tragedy. Cadmium does not glow.
  - Concern: HIGH — exact match to the Radium row's fact_a_true; the most classic mis-attribution in the set.

## Element 49 — In — Indium
- **fact_a_true:** "Indium makes touchscreens work so you can tap your tablet and phone."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Indium tin oxide (ITO) is the transparent conductive coating on virtually all capacitive touchscreens.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Most of the air we breathe is Indium, even more than oxygen."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Nitrogen)
  - Reason: Air is ~78% nitrogen (row 7), ~21% oxygen. Indium is a trace metal, not a gas. The claim is also categorically wrong because In is a solid.
  - Concern: LOW — so absurd ("breathing a metal") that a kid is more likely to giggle than internalize it; less mis-teachable than the plausible swaps.

## Element 50 — Sn — Tin
- **fact_a_true:** "Tin coats steel cans so the soup or beans inside don't rust the can."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: "Tin cans" are tin-plated steel; the tin layer protects the steel from corrosion by food acids.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Tin makes the tiny gold tip on fancy fountain pens last extra long."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Ruthenium / PGM tipping)
  - Reason: Fountain pen nib tipping is platinum-group metal alloy (Os/Ru/Ir), not tin. Tin is too soft.
  - Concern: MED — plausible because tin is also a soft shiny metal; could swap with the Ru fact.

## Element 51 — Sb — Antimony
- **fact_a_true:** "Antimony helps make fire-resistant clothes that don't catch fire easily."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Antimony trioxide (Sb2O3) is the most widely used flame-retardant synergist in textiles, plastics, and electronics housings.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Antimony is one of the few elements whose name comes from a Greek word for a green twig."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Thallium)
  - Reason: Thallium (row 81) is named from Greek "thallos" meaning a green shoot/twig (because of its bright green flame test). Antimony's name comes from "stibium" (Latin from Greek "stimmi"), unrelated to twigs.
  - Concern: LOW — etymology is too abstract for a 4yo to remember either way; low mis-teaching impact.

## Element 52 — Te — Tellurium
- **fact_a_true:** "Tellurium helps solar panels turn sunshine into power for homes."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Cadmium telluride (CdTe) is the second-most-common photovoltaic technology after crystalline silicon, used by First Solar and others.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Tellurium is the heaviest element ever made and is named after Yuri Oganessian."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Oganesson)
  - Reason: Oganesson (row 118, Og) is the heaviest synthesized element, named for Yuri Oganessian. Tellurium is element 52, named from Latin "tellus" (earth), and occurs naturally.
  - Concern: LOW — the names are very different and the kid likely hasn't met Og yet; minimal swap risk.

## Element 53 — I — Iodine
- **fact_a_true:** "Iodine is added to table salt to help your body grow strong."
  - Verdict: TRUE (slightly imprecise but acceptable)
  - Reason: Iodized salt is the public-health intervention for preventing iodine-deficiency goiter and supporting thyroid hormone production. "Grow strong" is a kid-level simplification of thyroid function.
  - Concern: LOW — fine for a 4yo.
- **fact_b_false:** "Iodine is named for its ruby-red color when it burns in a flame."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Rubidium)
  - Reason: Rubidium (row 37) is named from Latin "rubidus" (deep red) for its flame color. Iodine is named from Greek "iodes" (violet) for its violet vapor.
  - Concern: LOW — kid won't track the etymology either way.

## Element 54 — Xe — Xenon
- **fact_a_true:** "Xenon glows bright white in flash bulbs and powers ion engines on space probes."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Xenon flash lamps are standard in photography flashes and stroboscopes; Xe is the standard propellant for Hall-effect / gridded ion thrusters (e.g. Dawn, Hayabusa, BepiColombo).
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Xenon has the highest melting point of any metal, which is why it's in light-bulb wires."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Tungsten + category error)
  - Reason: Tungsten (row 74) has the highest melting point of any metal and is the light-bulb filament. Xenon is a noble GAS, not a metal — so the claim is doubly wrong.
  - Concern: MED — the "in light bulbs" part is mildly true (Xe is in some specialty lamps), making the wider claim feel half-right. Mis-attributable to tungsten.

## Element 55 — Cs — Cesium
- **fact_a_true:** "Cesium runs the most accurate clocks in the world, losing only a second every millions of years."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: The SI second is defined by the cesium-133 hyperfine transition; cesium fountain clocks lose ~1 second per ~100 million years. "Millions of years" is a kid-acceptable rounding.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Cesium is named after Wilhelm Roentgen, who discovered X-rays."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Roentgenium)
  - Reason: Roentgenium (row 111, Rg) is named for Roentgen. Cesium is named from Latin "caesius" (sky-blue) for its flame-test color.
  - Concern: LOW — kid won't retain either etymology.

## Element 56 — Ba — Barium
- **fact_a_true:** "Barium makes fireworks burn bright green and helps doctors take pictures of stomachs."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Barium salts (Ba(NO3)2, BaCl2) give green flame color in pyrotechnics; barium sulfate "barium meal/swallow" is the X-ray contrast agent for GI imaging.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Barium keeps cuts and scrapes from getting sore and also makes pennies feel light."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Zinc)
  - Reason: Zinc (row 30) is the cuts/scrapes (zinc oxide ointment) AND penny (US pennies are 97.5% Zn) element. Barium compounds are mostly toxic and would NOT be applied to wounds.
  - Concern: HIGH — the "put it on cuts" framing could put a kid in real danger if generalized. Even though they're not going to handle Ba directly, mis-teaching that exotic metals are wound-safe is a bad pattern.

## Element 57 — La — Lanthanum
- **fact_a_true:** "Lanthanum makes camera lenses sharper so pictures look crisp and clear."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Lanthanum oxide is added to optical glass (lanthanum crown glass) to produce high refractive index with low dispersion — sharper lenses with less chromatic aberration. Used in premium camera lenses.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Lanthanum creates the strongest magnetic fields of any natural element."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution; closest match is Holmium)
  - Reason: Holmium (row 67) has the highest magnetic moment of any naturally occurring element and is used in pole pieces of the strongest static magnets. Lanthanum is not magnetically notable. (Aside: the strongest PERMANENT magnets are Nd-based — row 60 — but Nd's strength comes from the alloy, not the element alone.)
  - Concern: MED — plausible-sounding but Ho is too rare to be a common-knowledge clash.

## Element 58 — Ce — Cerium
- **fact_a_true:** "Cerium polishes glass to a mirror finish and is the most common rare-earth metal."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: Cerium oxide (CeO2, "jeweler's rouge" variant) is the industry-standard glass-polishing compound. Ce is the most abundant of the 17 rare-earth elements (~66 ppm in crust, more common than copper).
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Cerium is named after Ernest Rutherford, who discovered the center of the atom."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Rutherfordium)
  - Reason: Rutherfordium (row 104, Rf) is named for Rutherford. Cerium is named for the asteroid Ceres (discovered 1801, same year Ce was isolated).
  - Concern: LOW — etymology, kid won't track.

## Element 59 — Pr — Praseodymium
- **fact_a_true:** "Praseodymium colors special goggles green so welders can see safely through bright sparks."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: "Didymium" glass (a Pr-Nd oxide mixture, historically inseparable, now intentionally blended) is used in glassblowers' and welders' safety goggles to filter out the bright yellow sodium-flare light. The glass appears green-tinted.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Praseodymium makes steel tools extra tough, like the wrenches in a car mechanic's shop."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Vanadium)
  - Reason: Vanadium (row 23) is THE classic tool-steel toughening alloy ("chrome-vanadium" wrenches). Pr is occasionally added to specialty steels but has no everyday tool-steel role.
  - Concern: MED — plausible swap with vanadium; same exact phrasing as the Vanadium row.

## Element 60 — Nd — Neodymium
- **fact_a_true:** "Neodymium makes the strongest magnets on Earth, found in headphones and hard drives."
  - Verdict: TRUE
  - Reason: NdFeB (neodymium-iron-boron) permanent magnets are the strongest commercial permanent magnets; ubiquitous in headphone drivers, HDD voice coils, EV motors, wind turbines.
  - Concern: NONE
- **fact_b_false:** "Neodymium is named after a hero from Greek myths who gave fire to people."
  - Verdict: FALSE (mis-attribution to Promethium)
  - Reason: Promethium (row 61, Pm) is named for Prometheus. Neodymium's name comes from Greek "neos didymos" = "new twin" (because it was a "twin" element separated from didymium alongside praseodymium).
  - Concern: LOW — kid unlikely to internalize Greek etymology.

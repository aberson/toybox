# Investigation: elements 61-80 (Promethium → Mercury)

## #61 Promethium (Pm-61)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Promethium is named after a hero from Greek myths who gave fire to people.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Pm is named after Prometheus, the Titan who stole fire for humanity. Age-appropriate and accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Promethium is named after Berkeley, California, where scientists first made it.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Berkelium (Bk-97)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Promethium was first isolated at Oak Ridge, not Berkeley. (Minor nit: Pm has been claimed in earlier work too, but the Berkeley attribution belongs unambiguously to Berkelium.)

## #62 Samarium (Sm-62)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Samarium makes powerful magnets that still work even when very hot.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Samarium-cobalt (SmCo) magnets are specifically prized for high-temperature stability (used in aerospace, military). Solid kid-level claim.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Samarium is the bright yellow stuff near volcanoes, and it smells like rotten eggs.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Sulfur (S-16)
- Notes: Clean recycle — the volcano-yellow-rotten-eggs description is canonically sulfur.

## #63 Europium (Eu-63)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Europium makes the red color in TV screens and the glow in some banknotes.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Eu³⁺ phosphors give the red in CRT/LED displays, and europium complexes are used as anti-counterfeit fluorescent markers in Euro banknotes. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Europium is added to table salt to help your body grow strong.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: not true of any element — but iodine (I-53) is the element added to table salt
- Notes: A 4yo who hears this could conflate it with iodized salt, since iodine fortification of salt is genuinely a thing. The "helps your body grow strong" phrasing also sounds like a nutrition claim. Consider rewording or replacing.

## #64 Gadolinium (Gd-64)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Gadolinium helps doctors take clearer pictures inside the body with MRI scans.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Gadolinium-based contrast agents are standard in MRI imaging. Kid-appropriate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Gadolinium is named after Thor, the Norse god of thunder.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Thorium (Th-90)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Gadolinium is actually named after chemist Johan Gadolin.

## #65 Terbium (Tb-65)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Terbium makes the bright green color you see on TV and tablet screens.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Tb³⁺ is a standard green phosphor in displays and fluorescent lamps. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Terbium helps make toothpaste that keeps your teeth strong.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: not true of any element — but fluorine (F-9) / fluoride is in toothpaste
- Notes: Kids hear "toothpaste makes teeth strong" constantly, so a 4yo could lock this in. Better to swap to a clearly-belongs-to-another-element distractor (e.g., recycle a fluorine fact).

## #66 Dysprosium (Dy-66)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Dysprosium makes magnets that don't lose their pull even when very hot.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Dy is added to NdFeB magnets to retain coercivity at elevated temperatures (EVs, wind turbines). Accurate. Slight overlap with the Samarium fact (#62 fact_a) — both true, but kids may find them indistinguishable.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Dysprosium cleans up car exhaust so the air stays cleaner to breathe.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Platinum (Pt-78), Palladium (Pd-46), or Rhodium (Rh-45) — the catalytic converter trio
- Notes: Catalytic converters genuinely do this, just not with Dy. Mildly mis-teachable since the function is real; a kid won't realize the wrong element is named.

## #67 Holmium (Ho-67)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Holmium creates the strongest magnetic fields of any natural element.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Ho has the highest magnetic moment per atom of any naturally occurring element (~10.6 μB) and is used in magnet pole pieces to concentrate flux. Accurate, though "strongest magnetic fields" is a slight simplification — more precisely, highest atomic magnetic moment.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Holmium grows into beautiful rainbow staircase crystals that look like tiny castles.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Bismuth (Bi-83)
- Notes: Clean recycle — the iridescent hopper/staircase crystals are canonically bismuth.

## #68 Erbium (Er-68)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Erbium colors glass and ceramics a pretty pink and helps light travel through cables.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Er gives glass a pink/rose tint and erbium-doped fiber amplifiers (EDFAs) are the backbone of long-haul fiber optics. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Erbium makes balloons float and makes voices sound funny when you breathe it in.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Helium (He-2)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Erbium is a solid metal at room temperature; the absurdity may not register with a 4yo, but the helium attribution is unmistakable.

## #69 Thulium (Tm-69)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Thulium glows blue in some special lasers and was once the rarest of the rare earths.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Tm:YAG and Tm-doped fiber lasers emit in blue/green-blue and ~2 μm IR; Tm was historically considered the rarest stable lanthanide (Pm is rarer but radioactive and not naturally occurring in usable amounts). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Thulium helps make the bright red dots on old TV screens.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Europium (Eu-63)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Pairs naturally with #63 fact_a.

## #70 Ytterbium (Yb-70)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Ytterbium helps make atomic clocks that keep time more precisely than ever before.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Yb optical lattice clocks are state-of-the-art (NIST, RIKEN), now more precise than cesium. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Ytterbium is the densest natural element, so a small chunk feels surprisingly heavy.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Osmium (Os-76)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Yb is actually quite low-density for a lanthanide (~6.9 g/cm³).

## #71 Lutetium (Lu-71)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Lutetium is one of the rarest and most expensive metals to dig out of the ground.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Lu is generally the most expensive of the lanthanides due to low crustal abundance and difficult separation. Accurate at kid level.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Lutetium is named after Nihon, which is the Japanese word for Japan.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Nihonium (Nh-113)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Lutetium is actually named after Lutetia, the Roman name for Paris.

## #72 Hafnium (Hf-72)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Hafnium makes super-fast computer chips run cooler and use less power.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Hafnium dioxide (HfO₂) replaced silicon dioxide as the high-k gate dielectric in CMOS chips starting ~45nm node (Intel 2007). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Hafnium was used in the very first transistors that started the computer age.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Germanium (Ge-32) — the first transistor (Bell Labs, 1947) was germanium
- Notes: A child won't have the context to know hafnium is a modern-chip element. The "started the computer age" framing makes this sound plausible and historically important — exactly the kind of false fact that sticks.

## #73 Tantalum (Ta-73)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Tantalum stores power inside phones, tablets, and game consoles in tiny capacitors.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Tantalum capacitors are a major electronics component. Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Tantalum glows blue in some special lasers and was once the rarest of the rare earths.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Thulium (Tm-69)
- Notes: Clean recycle from #69 fact_a. Internally inconsistent too — Ta is not a rare earth — but a 4yo won't catch that. Safe distractor.

## #74 Tungsten (W-74)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Tungsten has the highest melting point of any metal, which is why it's in light-bulb wires.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: W melts at ~3422°C, highest of any pure metal. Incandescent bulb filaments. Accurate. (Carbon technically has a higher sublimation point but is not a metal.)

**fact_b (claimed false):** Tungsten is named after Glenn Seaborg, who discovered many heavy elements.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Seaborgium (Sg-106)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Tungsten's name is Swedish ("heavy stone"); its symbol W comes from "Wolfram."

## #75 Rhenium (Re-75)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Rhenium helps jet engines spin hot and fast without melting.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Re is a key alloying element in nickel-based single-crystal superalloys for turbine blades (CMSX-4, etc.). Accurate. Note this duplicates the Lithium fact_b (#3 fact_b claims Li does this — which is the false one — so consistent).

**fact_b (claimed false):** Rhenium helps batteries work and gives some glass a pretty purple color.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Manganese (Mn-25) — alkaline batteries use MnO₂, and Mn gives glass a purple/pink hue (used as a "decolorizer" historically)
- Notes: Clean recycle from a different element's true fact. Combining battery + purple-glass narrows it to Mn.

## #76 Osmium (Os-76)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Osmium is the densest natural element, so a small chunk feels surprisingly heavy.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Os at ~22.59 g/cm³ is the densest naturally occurring element (Ir is very close). Accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Osmium makes powerful magnets that still work even when very hot.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Samarium (Sm-62)
- Notes: Clean recycle from #62 fact_a.

## #77 Iridium (Ir-77)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Iridium is so tough that scientists use it to mark the original meter standard.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: The International Prototype Metre (1889) and Kilogram were 90% Pt / 10% Ir alloy, chosen for chemical inertness and rigidity. Slight simplification — Pt provides the bulk and Ir adds hardness — but core claim is accurate.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Iridium is named after Albert Einstein, the famous scientist who imagined time bending.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Einsteinium (Es-99)
- Notes: Clean recycle. Iridium is actually named after Iris, the Greek rainbow goddess (its salts come in many colors).

## #78 Platinum (Pt-78)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Platinum is so rare that even less of it exists than gold, and it makes fancy wedding rings.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Crustal abundance of Pt (~0.005 ppm) is lower than Au (~0.004 ppm) — they're comparably rare with Pt usually cited as slightly rarer. Annual mined Pt is also much less than Au. Wedding rings are accurate. Solid claim.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Platinum is a quiet gas that fills old light bulbs to keep them from burning out.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Argon (Ar-18) — or Krypton — the noble gas fill in incandescent bulbs
- Notes: Clean recycle. The absurdity ("platinum is a gas") gives a savvy listener an out, but a 4yo just hears "fills light bulbs" and might attach it to Pt. Acceptable since it pairs with a real fill-gas fact elsewhere.

## #79 Gold (Au-79)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Gold is so soft you can hammer it into sheets thin enough to see through.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Gold leaf can be beaten to ~100 nm thick, becoming translucent (greenish/blue transmitted light). Classic accurate fact.

**fact_b (claimed false):** Gold runs the most accurate clocks in the world, losing only a second every millions of years.
- Verdict: MIS-TEACHABLE
- Belongs to: Cesium (Cs-55) — the SI definition of the second is the Cs-133 hyperfine transition; also overlaps with Ytterbium (#70 fact_a)
- Notes: Atomic clocks are real and exactly that precise, so the kid hears a real claim attached to a real-sounding element. Gold IS used decoratively in clock housings, which could compound confusion. Slightly more dangerous than a typical recycle.

## #80 Mercury (Hg-80)

**fact_a (claimed true):** Mercury is the only metal that's liquid at room temperature, like silver water.
- Verdict: TRUE
- Notes: Hg is the only metal liquid at standard room temperature (~25°C). Gallium melts at 30°C, Cesium at 28.5°C, Francium estimated ~27°C — none liquid at room temp by convention. "Silver water" is an evocative kid-friendly description. (No safety note here, but worth flagging that Hg is toxic — though the fact itself is fine.)

**fact_b (claimed false):** Mercury is named after the planet Uranus and powers nuclear reactors that make electricity.
- Verdict: FALSE (correctly)
- Belongs to: Uranium (U-92) on both counts — named after Uranus AND powers nuclear reactors
- Notes: Double recycle in one sentence, both pointing to U. Clean. Mercury is actually named after the planet/Roman god Mercury, so a kid might get confused about WHICH planet — but the conjunction with "nuclear reactors" makes the falsehood obvious to anyone who knows uranium.

## Summary flags for operator review

The four most mis-teachable entries in this range (worth editing before promoting to `source: operator`):

- **#63 Europium fact_b** — "added to table salt" overlaps with iodized salt, a real childhood-nutrition fact.
- **#65 Terbium fact_b** — "toothpaste keeps teeth strong" parrots a real fluoride claim kids hear constantly.
- **#72 Hafnium fact_b** — "first transistors" sounds like a real historical claim; a kid won't have context to flag it.
- **#79 Gold fact_b** — atomic-clock precision is a real claim and gold's association with luxury/precision instruments makes it stick.

The remaining 16 entries are clean recycles where the false fact unambiguously belongs to a different named element and is unlikely to cause persistent mislearning.
